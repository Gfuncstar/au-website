Infection control is the area where a small gap turns into a regulatory event fastest. The two questions that matter here are: do you have a written procedure, and could you produce it on the day an inspector or insurer asked. "I follow good practice" is not an answer. A document is.

## Checklist

- **Written infection-control procedures** covering cleaning, hand hygiene, surface disinfection, and instrument decontamination
- **Sharps bins** in clinic, BS 7320 compliant, lid date-recorded
- **Clinical waste contract** with a licensed carrier, collection records on file
- **Written complication management plan** specific to the procedures you perform
- **Emergency procedures** including anaphylaxis response, vascular occlusion protocol, and the local A&E referral path
- **Adverse event log** kept up to date, escalation / referral pathway documented

## Notes

- An infection-control policy doesn't need to be long. One side of A4 covering hand hygiene, surface cleaning between clients, single-use vs reusable instruments, and waste disposal is sufficient if it accurately describes what you actually do.
- Sharps bins must be British Standard BS 7320. The lid carries the open date and the close date. Sharps bins left in clinic for longer than three months without being closed off are a common audit fail.
- Vascular occlusion protocol is the document an aesthetic regulator will single out. It needs to name the prescriber, the stock dilution, the escalation contact, and the ophthalmology referral path. See the [vascular occlusion protocol](https://aestheticsunlocked.co.uk/blog/vascular-occlusion-hyaluronic-acid-filler-uk) on the Journal for the framework.
- An adverse event log doesn't mean adverse events have happened. It means you have a place to record one if it does, with date, client, treatment, response, and follow-up.

## Where to source

- Public Health England infection-prevention guidance
- Manufacturer adverse-event reporting forms
- Your indemnity insurer's complication-management template
- The JCCP Code of Practice for emergency-protocol standards

If you are working with prescription-only injectables (toxin, hyalase, lidocaine) without a written complication management plan and a named prescriber on file, do that today before you do anything else on this audit.
