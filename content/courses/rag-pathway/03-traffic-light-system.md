The Traffic Light System is the **navigational backbone** of the entire RAG Pathway. It gives you a shared language for assessing where any decision, a treatment, a piece of marketing, a training certificate, a premises set-up, sits on the spectrum from clear professional risk through to demonstrable best practice.

By the end of this module you will be able to identify what puts your licence, insurance, or reputation at risk (Red), what requires improvement, evidence, or clarification (Amber), and what demonstrates the kind of professional maturity that protects and grows your reputation (Green).

The framework is built around the four elements that regulators, insurers, and complaint-handlers actually assess: **risk, competence, premises, and oversight**. Running through everything is the principle that anchors the whole programme, *if you cannot justify it on paper, you cannot defend it in practice.*

## Where the system comes from

The Traffic Light System originates from the UK Government's response to the **Keogh Review** and subsequent patient-safety work around non-surgical cosmetic procedures. It now aligns with:

- Department of Health & Social Care (DHSC) policy direction
- The Health and Care Act 2022 (England)
- The proposed national licensing scheme for non-surgical cosmetic procedures
- Risk-based regulation principles used across healthcare

It is not owned by one body. It is the way modern regulators already think, and the framework that future statutory licensing is expected to be built on.

**Important:** The licensing scheme is pending in law, but the risk framework is already active in practice. Councils, insurers, the CQC, and the ASA are already making decisions on a Red–Amber–Green logic, even where the words are not used out loud.

## 1. Risk, the first lens

Every treatment carries a risk profile. The Traffic Light System sorts that risk into three bands.

### Red, high risk

Treatments where harm, if it occurs, is significant, hard to reverse, and may require medical intervention. They include:

- All injectables involving a Prescription-Only Medicine (botulinum toxin, dermal fillers supplied via prescription, polynucleotides, skin boosters)
- IV vitamin therapy and prescribed weight-management injectables
- Threadlifts and PDO threads
- Deep chemical peels (medical depth)
- Laser hair removal and ablative laser treatments in non-medical settings (regulated locally)

**Red expectations:** Statutory regulator oversight, regulated healthcare professional or supervised delegate, prescription supply chain, full clinical documentation, indemnity at the appropriate scope, premises that meet healthcare-grade infection-prevention standards.

### Amber, moderate risk

Treatments that are lower-risk than Red but still capable of causing harm if poorly performed, poorly documented, or performed outside scope. They include:

- Microneedling beyond superficial cosmetic depth
- Cosmetic-grade chemical peels above superficial depth
- Cosmetic radiofrequency, HIFU, and microcurrent in non-medical settings
- Skin-needling devices used at clinical depth
- LED phototherapy in clinical use

**Amber expectations:** Recognised qualifications mapped to the procedure, manufacturer training on devices, documented consent, robust contraindication screening, infection-prevention SOPs, premises licensing where the local authority requires it, evidenced CPD.

### Green, lower risk

Treatments that are well within the scope of a competent therapist and do not normally require regulated healthcare oversight. They include:

- Standard facials, superficial peels, mild microdermabrasion
- Cosmetic-grade LED, low-intensity radiofrequency
- Lash, brow, and skin-finishing services
- Aesthetic massage and lymphatic drainage

**Green expectations:** Recognised level-3/4 qualifications, manufacturer training where applicable, basic infection-prevention SOPs, public liability and treatment-specific insurance, consent and aftercare records.

## 2. Competence, what you can evidence

Competence is the second lens. The framework asks not "what can you do?" but "what can you **prove** you can do, safely, today?"

A defensible competence file contains:

- Original qualification certificates (with awarding body and date)
- Manufacturer device training certificates (with serial numbers where relevant)
- Supervised-practice logs for any procedure you trained in but did not previously perform
- Recent CPD relevant to each treatment offered (last 12 months)
- A scope-of-practice statement listing exactly what you do, and what you do not

**Why this matters:** When a complaint, an insurer query, or a regulatory request lands, the question is never "are you a kind person who tried hard?" It is "where is the evidence that you were trained, current, and within scope on the day of the treatment?"

## 3. Premises, where it happens

The same treatment performed in a council-licensed clinic with documented infection-prevention is not the same treatment performed on a kitchen table. Premises is the third lens, and councils are already enforcing it.

The framework expects you to know:

- Whether your local authority requires registration or licensing for the treatments you provide
- Whether any treatment on your menu meets the **CQC regulated-activity threshold**
- That mobile and home-visit work has tighter premises requirements, not looser ones
- That sub-letting a chair or treatment room does not transfer regulatory responsibility, both parties are accountable

**Best practice:** Keep a single premises file containing your council registration (or evidence it isn't required), your insurance schedule for the address, your infection-prevention SOPs, your sharps and waste contracts, and your last-inspection record.

## 4. Oversight, the governance layer

The fourth lens is the one most often missing in solo practice: who is checking your work, and how often?

Strong oversight evidence includes:

- A named prescriber for any POM-based treatment, with documented face-to-face consultation arrangements
- A clinical supervisor or peer-review buddy for case discussion (especially for complex skin)
- Audit cycles, e.g. quarterly review of consent forms, complications, and aftercare outcomes
- Documented adverse-event reporting (Yellow Card for medicines, MHRA reporting for devices)
- An incident log, even when nothing has gone wrong, the log itself is evidence of culture

**Key point:** Oversight is not a sign that you doubt yourself. It is the single clearest signal, to insurers, regulators, and clients, that you are a serious clinician operating a serious practice.

## The biggest mistake, misclassification

The most common professional failure in this industry is **misclassifying a treatment as lower-risk than it actually is**.

Misclassification usually sounds like:

- "It's basically the same as a facial."
- "It's not really medical."
- "I only do a tiny amount."
- "It's just a skin booster, not a filler."
- "Everyone else is doing it this way."

**Important:** Risk does not reduce because you minimise it in language. If a treatment sits in Amber or Red territory, describing it as Green will not protect you when an adverse event, a complaint, or an inspection arrives. The classification is the regulator's, not yours.

## How to use the framework on yourself

Take any treatment on your current menu and walk it through four questions:

1. **Risk**, Where does this sit? Red, Amber, or Green?
2. **Competence**, What can I evidence today that proves I am trained, current, and in scope for this?
3. **Premises**, Does where I deliver this meet the standards expected for this risk band?
4. **Oversight**, Who is checking this work, and how often is it documented?

A defensible treatment scores cleanly on all four. A risky one scores cleanly on one or two. A red-flag one scores cleanly on none, and that is the work this programme is designed to surface and resolve.
