This module maps out the **recognised UK training and qualification landscape** so you can place your own training history on it honestly. It covers the recognised UK frameworks (Level 4, Level 6, Level 7), the approved qualification pathways emerging from the DHSC consultation, CPD requirements and how to record them, the main membership bodies (JCCP, BCAM, BACN, ACE Group and others), supervised-practice expectations, and the often-missed link between your qualifications and the indemnity cover you can actually rely on.

By the end you will know what your **next credible training step** is — and why it matters for both your scope and your insurance.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Place your own training honestly on the recognised UK framework
- Identify the qualification pathways most likely to map into the future licensing scheme
- Build a CPD record that meets your regulator and your insurer
- Choose the membership bodies that strengthen your defensibility
- Plan the next training step with eyes open about cost, time, and value

## 1. The recognised UK qualification framework

UK education and skills qualifications are mapped to the **Regulated Qualifications Framework (RQF)**. In aesthetics, three RQF levels carry meaningful weight:

**Level 3** — basic beauty therapy, lash, brow, and skin-finishing services. The floor for entry-level non-medical practice. Not normally sufficient on its own for cosmetic injectables, advanced peels, or invasive needling.

**Level 4** — beauty / aesthetic therapy at a practitioner level. Currently the recognised baseline for many Green and lower-Amber treatments delivered by non-medical practitioners (cosmetic peels, microneedling at non-clinical depth, advanced facials).

**Level 5** — practitioner-level qualifications in laser / IPL and certain device categories, where required by the local authority and / or insurer.

**Level 6** — degree-equivalent. Often required (or strongly preferred) for non-medical practitioners performing cosmetic injectables, where the indemnity market accepts non-medical providers at all.

**Level 7** — postgraduate-equivalent. Increasingly the expected level for practitioners delivering injectable cosmetic procedures, particularly in the move toward the proposed national licensing scheme. Many indemnity providers and prescribers now require Level 7 for ongoing injectable cover.

**Important:** A "Level 7 course" is meaningless on its own. The qualification is only as credible as the **awarding body** behind it (e.g. OFQUAL-regulated awarding organisations, university-validated programmes). One-day courses calling themselves "Level 7" without a regulated awarding body do not carry the same recognition.

## 2. The DHSC direction — what the licensing scheme is likely to expect

The 2023 DHSC consultation proposed minimum training and qualification thresholds tied to the three-tier (green / amber / red) procedure model:

- **Green-tier procedures** — recognised qualifications appropriate to the treatment, plus manufacturer training and CPD
- **Amber-tier procedures** — Level 4 / 5 minimums likely, with specific training mapped to the procedure and supervised practice on the way to autonomous delivery
- **Red-tier procedures** — restricted to registered healthcare professionals, with Level 6 / 7 expected as the baseline and supervised practice for any new procedure

**Why this matters:** The licensing scheme has not been finalised, but the trajectory is set. Practitioners already operating at Level 6 / 7 with formal supervised-practice logs will move into licensing with minimum disruption. Practitioners relying on shorter, unmapped training will face the steepest re-qualification climb.

## 3. Supervised practice — the bridge from qualified to competent

Supervised practice is the part of training most often skipped — and the part most heavily weighted by insurers, prescribers, and future licensing.

A defensible supervised-practice log records:

- **Procedure** — by name and risk band
- **Date and patient identifier** (anonymised)
- **Supervisor** — named, with their qualification and registration
- **Mode of supervision** — direct observation, indirect with case discussion, sign-off only
- **Sign-off criteria met** — explicit, against a competence framework
- **Reflection** — what was learned, what would change next time

**Best practice:** Treat supervised practice as a structured curriculum, not a ticking-off exercise. Aim for a defined number of cases per procedure (typical ranges: 10–25 for new injectables, 5–10 for new device-based procedures) before moving to autonomous practice.

## 4. CPD — current, relevant, evidenced

CPD requirements vary by registration body — NMC currently requires 35 hours over three years, of which 20 must be participatory; the GMC, GPhC, and GDC each set their own. The headline principle is the same across all of them: your CPD must be **current, relevant to your scope, and evidenced**.

A strong CPD file contains:

- A **rolling 12-month** log of activity (date, type, source, hours)
- **Reflective notes** — what was learned, how it changed practice, what the patient impact was
- **Manufacturer certificates** — for every device, product, and technique on site
- **Complex-case learning** — peer review, journal clubs, case discussion
- **A scope-of-practice statement** that maps each treatment to the CPD that supports it

**Common mistakes:**

- Recording attendance without reflection — "attended a 2-hour webinar" with no learning outcome
- Logging only formal courses, not informal CPD (peer review, supervised cases, mentoring)
- Letting the file go stale — a CPD record that hasn't been updated in 18 months is functionally invisible
- CPD that doesn't match the menu — strong injectables CPD on a clinic that primarily does device work, or vice versa

## 5. Membership bodies — what they add

Voluntary membership bodies do not replace your statutory regulator, but they strengthen your defensibility. The main ones in non-surgical aesthetics:

**JCCP (Joint Council for Cosmetic Practitioners)** — PSA-accredited voluntary register. Open to practitioners across professional backgrounds. Membership requires evidenced training, indemnity, supervision, and CPD. The closest thing to a unified non-surgical standard in England.

**CPSA (Cosmetic Practice Standards Authority)** — sets the competency standards used by the JCCP register. Practitioner standards mapped to procedure and risk.

**BCAM (British College of Aesthetic Medicine)** — for doctors. Strong on CPD, peer review, and scientific rigour.

**BACN (British Association of Cosmetic Nurses)** — for registered nurses. Strong on advocacy, peer support, and CPD.

**ACE Group World** — multidisciplinary group with a strong emergency-management focus (vascular occlusion, anaphylaxis). Membership benefit: rapid expert advice for adverse events.

**Save Face** — accreditation register for practitioners, focused on consumer protection. Visible to the public.

**Important:** Pick one or two bodies that genuinely match your practice and engage with them. Six logos in your Instagram bio with no engagement underneath them is weaker than two logos and a documented record of audit, peer review, and case discussion.

## 6. The training-to-indemnity link

The single most-overlooked piece of evidence in many practitioners' files is the **link between their qualifications and their indemnity policy**. Insurers rate cover on:

- The **awarding body** of the qualification
- The **level** of the qualification (RQF Level)
- The **recency** of the qualification — many policies require a CPD top-up within the last 12–24 months
- **Manufacturer training** for each named device or product
- The **delivery model** (clinic, mobile, home-visit) declared at policy inception

A common pattern of failure looks like this:

1. Practitioner trains on Brand A device, gets cover
2. Switches to Brand B device, never tells the insurer
3. Treats a patient with Brand B, adverse event occurs
4. Insurer refuses cover because Brand B was not declared and manufacturer training is not on file

**Best practice:** Every time you add a treatment, change a device, change a product, or upgrade your qualification, **email your insurer the same week**. Update the schedule. Keep the email trail.

## 7. Choosing your next training step

When choosing the next course, run the decision through five questions:

1. **Awarding body** — is it OFQUAL-regulated, university-validated, or manufacturer-only? (Hierarchy matters)
2. **Level** — does it formally map onto the RQF, or is "Level 7" used as a marketing label only?
3. **Supervised practice** — does the course include a structured supervised-practice element, or is it knowledge-only?
4. **Insurance impact** — does completing this course move your indemnity offer forward (lower premium, broader scope, recognised provider)?
5. **Scope match** — is it filling an actual gap in your practice, or is it an interesting course for its own sake?

**Why this matters:** Aesthetics CPD is expensive. Each training pound should be spent against a defensibility outcome — not a certificate to add to the wall.

## Reflection — your training file

Before the next module, build (or audit) your training file. It should contain:

- Original qualification certificates with awarding body, level, and date
- Manufacturer training for every device on site
- Supervised-practice log for any procedure trained-but-not-yet-autonomous
- Rolling 12-month CPD log with reflection notes
- Scope-of-practice statement aligned to the above
- Insurance schedule confirming cover for everything in the file
