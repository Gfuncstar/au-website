Clinical governance is what separates a one-off compliance push from a practice that **stays safe over time**. This module covers the governance frameworks that underpin good non-surgical practice, the documentation standards you should be working to, robust consent processes, adverse-event recording and reporting, audit cycles, CPD record-keeping, incident management, and how to work effectively with prescribers.

By the end of this module you will have a clearer picture of the small, repeated habits that quietly **demonstrate safety to insurers, regulators, and clients alike**.

## What clinical governance actually is

Clinical governance is the framework through which clinical services maintain and continuously improve quality and safety. It was first formalised in the NHS in the late 1990s and now underpins every regulated healthcare service in the UK. The same seven pillars apply to non-surgical aesthetic practice, at proportionate scale:

1. **Patient and public involvement**, informed consent, complaints handling, feedback
2. **Clinical effectiveness**, using interventions known to work, in the way they were designed to be used
3. **Risk management**, identifying, recording, and reducing harm
4. **Education and training**, current, evidenced CPD
5. **Audit**, routinely checking your own outcomes against your own standards
6. **Information and IT**, record-keeping, GDPR, secure storage
7. **Staff management**, scope, supervision, and competence assurance

**Key point:** Most solo practitioners never use the words "clinical governance". But every defensible solo practitioner is doing exactly this, quietly, on a smaller scale, every week.

## 1. Documentation standards, the clinical record

The clinical record is the single most important governance asset you control. A defensible record contains:

- **Consultation note**, presenting concern, history, expectations, contraindications screened, capacity considered
- **Photographic baseline**, consistent lighting, neutral expression, dated, consented
- **Treatment record**, product, batch number, expiry, dose / depth / duration, exact site and technique
- **Aftercare given**, verbal advice, written leaflet (version dated)
- **Consent**, signed, dated, version-controlled, with capacity and cooling-off period considered
- **Outcome and follow-up**, review timing, response, any adverse event
- **Communication**, every message, every call, every social-media DM relating to the treatment

**Best practice:** Use a single template per treatment so the record is uniform, complete, and impossible to forget a field on. Audit yourself every quarter, pull ten random records and check every field.

## 2. Consent, the gold standard

Consent is the legal anchor of every cosmetic intervention. Since the *Montgomery v Lanarkshire Health Board (2015)* judgment, the test is the **patient's** view of what is material, not the clinician's. For cosmetic practice this raises the bar significantly:

A defensible consent process includes:

- A face-to-face discussion of the treatment, alternatives, risks, and likely outcomes
- An honest discussion of what the treatment cannot achieve
- A documented cooling-off period (recommended minimum 24 hours for first-time injectables)
- Written consent on a treatment-specific form, version-dated
- A capacity check (and adjustments where needed)
- A clear note on under-18s, *which in England means a refusal to provide cosmetic injectables in any circumstances*
- A specific check on contraindications, pregnancy, breastfeeding, immunosuppression, recent vaccination, anti-coagulation, mental-health treatment

**Important:** Consent is a process, not a form. The form is evidence that the process happened, it is not the consent itself.

## 3. Adverse-event recording and reporting

Every clinic will have adverse events. The defensible clinic is the one that **records and learns** from them.

Build a simple adverse-event log with:

- Patient identifier, date, treatment, batch number
- Description of event (in clinical terms)
- Immediate management
- Escalation (prescriber, A&E, insurer)
- Resolution and time to resolution
- Reflection and any change to practice

**External reporting routes:**

- **Yellow Card scheme (MHRA)**, adverse reactions to medicines, suspected counterfeit medicines
- **MHRA medical device adverse-event reporting**, device failures or device-linked harm
- **Your indemnity provider**, anything that may give rise to a claim, irrespective of severity
- **JCCP (where you are a member)**, significant events under the Code of Practice
- **CQC (where registered)**, notifiable incidents

**Why this matters:** Most enforcement and litigation hinges on the post-event period. A clinic that documented the event, escalated appropriately, supported the patient, and changed practice in response is in a fundamentally different defensive position to one that did none of those things.

## 4. Audit, checking yourself

Audit is the simplest governance habit and the most rarely done. It does not have to be complex. A practical solo-practitioner audit cycle:

- **Quarterly:** pull 10 random clinical records and grade them for completeness against your template
- **Quarterly:** review your adverse-event log, patterns, themes, learning
- **Quarterly:** review one specific clinical area (e.g. consent forms, contraindication screening, photo quality)
- **Annually:** full review of SOPs, insurance schedule, prescriber agreement, premises documentation
- **Annually:** outcomes review, what worked, what didn't, what needs to come off the menu

**Best practice:** Date and sign every audit. The audit log itself is governance evidence, even when the findings are minor.

## 5. CPD, current, relevant, evidenced

CPD requirements vary by registration body, but the principle is universal: your training **at the point of treatment** is what matters, not the certificate from five years ago.

A strong CPD file contains:

- A 12-month rolling record of activity (hours, type, source, learning)
- Reflective notes, what you learned, how you applied it, what changed in practice
- Manufacturer training certificates for every device on site
- Evidence of complex-case learning (peer review, journal clubs, supervised practice logs)
- A scope-of-practice statement aligned to the CPD record

**Key point:** CPD is the bridge between "I qualified" and "I am still safe today". Insurers, prescribers, and future licensing will all look at the bridge, not the qualification at one end of it.

## 6. Incident management, when something goes wrong

Every clinic should have a simple incident-management protocol on the wall. The headline steps:

1. **Immediate clinical management**, patient safety first, escalate clinically
2. **Documentation**, write up the event in clinical terms, contemporaneously
3. **Notification**, prescriber, supervisor, insurer, and (where required) MHRA / CQC / JCCP
4. **Patient support**, honest, transparent communication, follow-up booked
5. **Reflection and change**, what changed in practice as a result

**Common mistakes:**

- Apologising in legally ambiguous terms before the clinical picture is clear (the duty of candour is to be open and honest, not to admit fault prematurely)
- Letting the insurer find out through the patient's solicitor
- Failing to record the event because "it resolved fine"
- Failing to support the patient because of fear of admitting fault

**Best practice:** A clear, contemporaneous record and a supportive patient relationship are the strongest defence available, far stronger than silence.

## 7. Working with prescribers, governance, not just supply

The prescriber relationship is a governance relationship, not a supply relationship. A working agreement includes:

- A face-to-face consultation pathway for every new patient
- Defined access to clinical records on both sides
- An agreed adverse-event escalation route, by name and number
- Joint review of complex cases (e.g. patients with multiple medical conditions)
- A shared record of CPD, scope, and indemnity for both parties

**Important clarification:** The prescriber's professional standards body, GMC, NMC, GPhC, applies to their conduct in *your* clinic. Your relationship with the prescriber is a governance relationship that is auditable by their regulator, not just your insurer.

## 8. Data protection, the quiet pillar

Most aesthetic practice handles **special-category personal data** (health data, biometric data, photographs of identifiable individuals). UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018 apply in full.

Your data-protection file should contain:

- A privacy notice given to every patient
- A defined lawful basis for each processing activity
- Consent records for marketing (separate from clinical consent)
- A retention schedule for clinical and photographic records (typically 8 years for adults, longer for under-18s)
- A breach log
- ICO registration as a data controller (a small annual fee)

**Why this matters:** Photographs and treatment notes are among the most sensitive personal data a person ever produces. A breach in this category is high-impact, high-fine, and high-reputational-damage.
