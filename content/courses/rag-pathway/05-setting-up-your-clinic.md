This module focuses on the **practical set-up** that the regulators, insurers, and local authorities will look at if they ever need to. It covers premises licensing as it currently stands and where it is heading, local authority and council requirements, infection-control standards, the documentation you need on file, how to structure your insurance correctly, the thresholds at which CQC registration becomes relevant, and how to work with a medical supervisor where applicable.

The aim is to give you a clear, sequenced checklist for setting up — or auditing — a clinic so that the **foundations match the reputation you are building on top of them**.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

- Decide whether your premises requires local authority registration today
- Build a documented infection-prevention SOP appropriate to your risk band
- Construct an insurance schedule that genuinely matches your menu
- Identify whether any treatment pulls you under CQC registration
- Set up a working relationship with a prescriber or medical supervisor
- Audit an existing clinic against the same checklist

## 1. Premises — registered, licensed, or neither?

Premises regulation in England is currently delivered by **local authorities** under by-laws and the Local Government (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1982. The position varies significantly by council, but three common categories apply:

- **Special treatment licence** — required by some councils (notably across Greater London) for many cosmetic services. Often covers premises, practitioner, and treatments separately.
- **Skin-piercing registration** — required for any treatment that breaks the skin (microneedling, electrolysis, micropigmentation) under most council schemes outside London.
- **No specific registration required** — for genuinely Green-band treatments in some local-authority areas, with the universal duty to comply with consumer, health-and-safety, and infection-control law still in place.

**Best practice:** Email or phone your local environmental health team and ask, in writing, which treatments on your menu require registration in your borough. Keep the response on file. The single biggest cause of council enforcement is operating a treatment that does require registration in a borough that does require it — without ever having checked.

## 2. The future — premises licensing under the Health and Care Act

The proposed national licensing scheme is expected to introduce **separate premises licensing** alongside practitioner licensing. The likely picture, based on the 2023 DHSC consultation:

- Premises licensed by the local authority against a national set of standards
- Standards calibrated to the highest-risk treatment offered on site
- Inspection, renewal, and revocation built into the scheme
- Mobile and home-based work tightened, not loosened

**Why this matters:** A clinic set up to current best-practice infection-prevention and documentation standards today will move into licensing without disruption. A clinic operating to the bare-minimum-for-today standard will face significant capital cost when licensing arrives.

## 3. Infection prevention — the SOP that defines you

Infection-prevention is the most-overlooked piece of evidence in non-surgical practice — and the one that most reliably separates Green-band clinics from Amber. Your SOP should cover:

**Hand hygiene and PPE**

- Hand-washing protocol (5 moments)
- Glove use, removal, and disposal
- Mask, apron, and eye protection where indicated

**Skin preparation**

- Alcohol vs aqueous antiseptic — which, when, and why
- Patch-test and contraindication checks before each session
- Pre-treatment photography and documentation

**Sharps and waste**

- Sharps bin compliance (BS 7320, EN ISO 23907)
- Clinical waste contract — quoted by name on the SOP
- Disposal records kept on file

**Equipment and surfaces**

- Single-use vs decontaminated tools — defined per treatment
- Decontamination cycle and validation (where autoclaving is used)
- Surface-cleaning schedule per session and end-of-day

**Sharps and injury management**

- Needle-stick injury protocol
- Source-patient testing pathway
- Reporting to RIDDOR (where applicable) and your prescriber

**Best practice:** Print the SOP, post the relevant sections in the relevant places (sink area, sharps bin, treatment trolley), and review the document every 12 months — with a date and signature. A document last updated three years ago looks worse than no document at all.

## 4. The clinic documentation file

Whether your clinic is one room or a multi-treatment site, the documentation file is the same. The folder — physical or digital — should contain:

- Council registration / licence (or written confirmation that registration is not required)
- Insurance schedule (full schedule, not just the certificate)
- Risk assessment for the premises (slips, trips, electrical, fire, lone working)
- Infection-prevention SOP (current version, dated)
- Sharps and waste contracts
- Equipment register (every device, with serial number, purchase date, manufacturer, and last service)
- Calibration / service log for any device that requires it
- Consent forms — current versions for every treatment
- Aftercare leaflets — current versions for every treatment
- Adverse-event log
- CPD records for every practitioner working on site
- Prescriber agreement (where applicable)
- Data protection / GDPR documentation (privacy notice, consent records, breach log)

**Why this matters:** When a complaint, an inspection, or an insurer query arrives, the question is rarely "did the practitioner do something wrong?" It is "what does the clinic's documentation show was the standard at the time?" The folder is the answer.

## 5. Insurance — built around your menu

We covered the principle in Module 3. In set-up, the practical work is:

- List every treatment you offer, by exact name
- Send the list to your insurer in writing
- Ask for a schedule that confirms cover for every named treatment
- Confirm cover for your delivery model (clinic, mobile, home-visit, multi-room)
- Confirm cover at your specific qualification level
- Confirm any device-specific requirements (manufacturer training, serial number)

**Common mistakes:**

- Adding a new treatment to the menu without notifying the insurer
- Using a parallel-import device the insurer doesn't recognise
- Letting the prescriber relationship drift to remote-only
- Operating from premises not declared on the policy

**Best practice:** Re-confirm the schedule annually and every time you change a treatment, a device, or a delivery model. Keep the email trail.

## 6. CQC — the scoping question

For most non-surgical practice, CQC registration is **not** required. But for a small and growing minority of clinics, it is — and the test is the activity, not the brand.

Run a simple scoping check:

1. Do you treat any condition (acne, rosacea, melasma, hyperhidrosis) as a **medical** rather than a **cosmetic** concern, in the way you describe and deliver the service?
2. Do you deliver any procedure that would normally be classified as **surgical** (e.g. mole removal, cyst removal, certain thread procedures)?
3. Do you provide any **diagnostic or screening** service delivered by a regulated healthcare professional?

If the answer to any of these is yes, CQC registration is likely required — and operating without it is a criminal offence.

**Important:** A single regulated activity can pull your whole service into CQC scope. If you are uncertain, the CQC scope-of-registration checker is the first stop, and written advice from a healthcare-regulatory adviser is the second.

## 7. Working with a prescriber or medical supervisor

If your menu includes any POM-based treatment, the prescriber relationship is non-negotiable — and structured correctly, it is one of your strongest defensibility assets.

A working prescriber relationship includes:

- A **face-to-face** consultation between prescriber and patient before any new POM-based treatment
- A documented prescription supplied through a registered pharmacy
- A defined arrangement for adverse-event escalation
- Mutual access to clinical records relevant to the treatment
- A written agreement covering scope, frequency of contact, and indemnity for both parties

**Important clarification:** Remote prescribing for cosmetic injectables is no longer accepted as best practice and is opposed by the GMC, NMC, GPhC, and CQC. Any practitioner relying on it should treat it as Amber moving to Red — and plan their transition accordingly.

A medical supervisor (where you do not employ a prescriber directly) plays a similar role for non-prescribing practitioners — peer review, complex-case discussion, clinical-governance oversight, and incident review.

## 8. Sequenced set-up checklist

If you are setting up — or auditing — a clinic, work in this order:

1. **Confirm premises status** with your local authority (in writing)
2. **Build the documentation file** — every item in section 4
3. **Write the infection-prevention SOP** appropriate to your risk band
4. **Issue the menu, treatment by treatment, to your insurer** — secure the schedule
5. **Run the CQC scoping check** — escalate to a regulatory adviser if unsure
6. **Set up the prescriber / supervisor agreement** — face-to-face, documented
7. **Photograph the clinic against the SOP** — proof-of-condition record
8. **Diary-mark a 12-month audit of the entire file**

**Best practice:** Treat the file as a living document. The point of clinical-grade set-up is not to pass an inspection once — it is to be inspection-ready every day.
