This module sets out the **legal and regulatory baseline** that applies to non-surgical aesthetic practice in England. It moves from the headline frameworks down to the practical questions that decide whether your practice is defensible: what your insurer is actually covering, where the boundary between medical and non-medical sits, and what the relevant bodies expect you to be able to evidence.

The aim is not to make you a lawyer. It is to make sure that **nothing on your current menu is operating outside a framework you don't know about**.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this module, you will clearly understand:

- The headline legal frameworks that apply in England
- The boundary between medical and non-medical aesthetics
- What your professional standards body expects you to evidence
- Insurance terms, exclusions, and the link to scope of practice
- JCCP guidance and what it adds to your defensibility
- MHRA expectations on devices and Prescription-Only Medicines
- The implications of the Health and Care Act 2022
- ASA / CAP advertising rules as they intersect with legal scope

## 1. The headline legal frameworks

There is no single Aesthetics Act. Instead, your practice sits inside a layered framework:

- **Health and Care Act 2022**, the enabling legislation for the future national licensing scheme
- **Medicines Act 1968 and the Human Medicines Regulations 2012**, control supply, prescribing, advertising and use of medicines
- **Care Quality Commission (Registration) Regulations 2009**, define regulated activities that require CQC registration
- **General Product Safety Regulations**, apply to devices and consumables not covered by the medical device regime
- **The Equality Act 2010, the Consumer Rights Act 2015, GDPR / UK Data Protection Act 2018**, apply universally to anyone offering services to the public
- **Local Government (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1982 (and council by-laws)**, local authority licensing of skin-piercing and special treatments
- **Botulinum Toxin and Cosmetic Fillers (Children) Act 2021**, bans the administration of these procedures to under-18s for cosmetic purposes in England

**Key point:** You don't need every section memorised. You need to know which of these apply to which of your treatments, and to be able to point to that evidence when asked.

## 2. The medical / non-medical boundary

This is the line that decides almost everything else.

A treatment crosses into **medical territory** when any of the following is true:

- It requires a Prescription-Only Medicine (POM), botulinum toxin, prescribed dermal fillers, polynucleotides, IV vitamins, weight-management injectables
- It breaks the skin to a clinical depth, beyond superficial cosmetic intent
- It is delivered using a device classified as a medical device (under MHRA / UK MDR regulations)
- It carries a clinical risk profile that would normally be managed by a regulated healthcare professional
- It treats a medical diagnosis (acne, rosacea, melasma) rather than a cosmetic concern

**Important clarification:** Marketing language does not change classification. Calling botulinum toxin a "wrinkle relaxer", or describing prescribed filler as a "skin booster", does not move the treatment out of the medical regime. The classification is set by the medicine, the device, or the depth, not the brochure.

## 3. Professional standards bodies, what they expect

If you are a registered healthcare professional, you operate under your regulator's standards on top of everything else in this module. The headline expectations:

**Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC):** Practise within scope. Maintain CPD relevant to aesthetics. Apply *The Code*, particularly around informed consent, capacity, honesty in advertising, and raising concerns. Be able to justify any treatment decision against the standards of a reasonable peer.

**General Medical Council (GMC):** *Good Medical Practice* applies in full to cosmetic practice. Specific guidance on cosmetic interventions covers consent, two-stage consent for surgical procedures, advertising, and the professional duty to refuse treatment that is not in the patient's interest.

**General Pharmaceutical Council (GPhC):** Standards for prescribing in cosmetic settings now require a face-to-face consultation between prescriber and patient. Remote prescribing of injectables for cosmetic use is no longer accepted as best practice and is opposed by all four healthcare regulators.

**General Dental Council (GDC):** Dentists working in non-surgical aesthetics must remain within their scope of practice and meet the same consent and advertising standards as the rest of the profession.

**Important:** If you are not on a statutory register, none of the above applies *to you*, but it absolutely applies to your prescriber, your supervising clinician, and anyone you work under. Their conduct standards become your operational floor by extension.

## 4. Insurance, the most-misread document in the industry

Most practitioners have insurance. Very few have read the schedule.

A defensible insurance file proves three things:

- **Cover for each named treatment**, every treatment on your menu must be listed, by name, on your schedule
- **Cover at the right qualification level**, many policies require a specific awarding body, level (4, 6, or 7), and recent CPD
- **No silent exclusions triggered by your delivery model**, mobile work, sub-letting, supervising students, and treating under-18s are common silent exclusions

Common ways practitioners discover their cover is invalid:

- Adding a new treatment to the menu without notifying the insurer
- Using a device the insurer doesn't recognise (often parallel-import devices)
- Treating outside the qualification level the policy was rated on
- Allowing the prescriber relationship to drift away from a face-to-face model
- Operating from premises not declared on the policy

**Why this matters:** Cover that "would have applied" is not cover. The moment you step outside the schedule, you are personally liable for the financial consequences of an adverse event, and your indemnity will not retrospectively step in.

## 5. JCCP and the voluntary register layer

The **Joint Council for Cosmetic Practitioners (JCCP)** is a Professional Standards Authority (PSA)-accredited voluntary register for non-surgical practitioners. It is not a statutory regulator. You can practise without it. But:

- JCCP membership signals to insurers, prescribers, and clients that you operate to a defined standard
- The JCCP Code of Practice and Practice Standards Authority (CPSA) competency framework are the closest thing England currently has to a unified non-surgical standard
- Future statutory licensing is widely expected to draw heavily on JCCP / CPSA competency mapping
- Membership requires evidence of training, indemnity, supervision, and CPD, which means the file you build for JCCP is the file you would build for licensing anyway

**Best practice:** Even if you choose not to join, build your evidence file *as if* you were applying. You will be ready when statutory licensing arrives, and your defensibility today is stronger.

## 6. MHRA, devices, medicines, and reporting

The **Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA)** is the statutory regulator for medicines and medical devices in the UK. Its remit touches non-surgical aesthetics in three places:

- **Medicines**, supply, advertising, prescribing, and patient safety reporting (Yellow Card scheme)
- **Medical devices**, CE / UKCA marking, parallel imports, post-market surveillance, and adverse-event reporting
- **Borderline products**, cosmetic ingredients that drift into medicinal claims (e.g. "treats", "cures", "regenerates")

**Red flags:**

- Using a device without UKCA / CE marking, or without manufacturer traceability
- Sourcing botulinum toxin or fillers from non-UK pharmacies without prescription oversight
- Advertising any POM by name (botulinum toxin / Botox / Azzalure / Bocouture), this is unlawful, irrespective of platform
- Failing to report a serious adverse event linked to a medicine or device

## 7. CQC, when it applies, when it doesn't

The **Care Quality Commission (CQC)** regulates healthcare services in England. Most cosmetic non-surgical practice does not meet the threshold for registration, but some does, and the test is the activity, not the title of the clinic.

CQC registration becomes relevant when your service includes:

- **Treatment of disease, disorder or injury (TDDI)** by a regulated healthcare professional, for example, treating acne or rosacea as a medical condition rather than a cosmetic concern
- **Surgical procedures** under regulated definitions
- **Diagnostic and screening procedures** delivered by a regulated professional

**Key point:** Adding a single regulated activity (often something that sounds clinical, like prescription acne management) can pull your whole service under CQC. If you are unsure, the CQC scope-of-registration checker is the first place to look, and a written opinion from a healthcare-regulatory adviser is the second.

## 8. The Health and Care Act 2022, what is coming

Section 180 of the Health and Care Act 2022 gives the Secretary of State power to introduce a national licensing scheme for non-surgical cosmetic procedures in England. The DHSC consultation in 2023 proposed:

- A three-tier procedure model (green / amber / red) based on clinical risk
- Premises licensing alongside practitioner licensing
- Minimum training and qualification thresholds for each tier
- Restrictions on who can perform red-tier procedures (likely registered healthcare professionals only)
- Local authority enforcement of the scheme

**Why this matters:** The licensing scheme is not yet in force, but the direction is set. Practitioners who already operate to the proposed standards will move into licensing without disruption. Practitioners operating at the bare-minimum-for-today level will face the steepest climb, and possibly the loss of treatments from their menu.

## 9. ASA / CAP, where law meets marketing

The **Advertising Standards Authority (ASA)** and **Committee of Advertising Practice (CAP)** enforce the UK advertising codes. Aesthetics is a high-risk advertising sector and the rules are applied strictly. The headline points:

- POMs (botulinum toxin and any branded equivalent) **cannot be advertised by name to the public**, on any platform
- Claims must be capable of objective substantiation, "amazing results" without evidence is non-compliant
- Before-and-after imagery must be representative, with informed consent and not misleadingly enhanced
- Influencer and gifted-content posts must be clearly labelled and must follow CAP rules
- Social media is treated as advertising, your grid, your stories, and your DMs are all in scope

We unpack the marketing rules in full in Module 6: for now, hold the principle: **what is unlawful to do is also unlawful to advertise**.
