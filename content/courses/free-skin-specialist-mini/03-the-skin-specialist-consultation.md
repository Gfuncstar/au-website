The Skin Specialist consultation is built around a simple principle: **structure surfaces what improvisation misses**.

It's a six-step flow. Each step has a specific clinical job. Skip a step and you create the consultation gap from the previous lesson.

## The six steps

### 1. Pre-consultation intake

Before the client walks in, you already have:

- **Demographic data** — age, hormonal stage (premenopausal, perimenopausal, postmenopausal), medications, pregnancy / breastfeeding status
- **Concern in their words** — what brought them in, what they've already tried, what hasn't worked
- **Photographic baseline** — taken in standardised lighting, neutral expression, consented for clinical use
- **Skin-type self-report** — useful as orientation, never as diagnosis

This intake is collected via a digital form before the session. **It saves 15 minutes of the consultation** and signals professionalism the moment they walk in.

### 2. Structured history

You ask the same questions in the same order, every time. The structure looks like this:

- **Onset** — when did this start? Sudden or gradual?
- **Duration** — how long has the current pattern been present?
- **Pattern over time** — better, worse, cyclical, stable?
- **Triggers** — what makes it worse? Sun, stress, alcohol, heat, hormones, skincare?
- **Previous treatments** — what's been tried? OTC, prescribed, in-clinic? What worked, what didn't, what made it worse?
- **Medical history** — relevant systemic conditions, current medications
- **Family history** — same condition or related (acne, rosacea, eczema, vitiligo, melasma) in close relatives

Every question has a clinical job. Trigger questions surface drivers. Previous-treatment questions surface barrier compromise. Family history surfaces predisposition. **Skip a question and you skip a layer of the picture.**

### 3. Structured examination

The examination is also patterned. You look at:

- **Distribution** — central face, T-zone, jawline, neck, back, chest. Distribution alone often points to the driver.
- **Lesion type** — comedones, papules, pustules, nodules, telangiectasia, scaling, plaques, post-inflammatory marks. Mixed lesion patterns matter.
- **Skin-tone classification** — Fitzpatrick I–VI. Affects treatment choice (PIH risk, laser settings, hydroquinone considerations).
- **Barrier baseline** — visible erythema, dehydration, scaling, sensitivity to touch. Even without a TEWL meter, a competent visual barrier assessment guides sequencing.
- **Photographic documentation** — same lighting, same angles, same expression, every visit. Foundational for both clinical progress tracking and defensible practice.

### 4. Classification

Now you name what you're looking at. This is where pattern recognition does the heavy lifting:

- **Condition** — acne / rosacea / pigmentation disorder / barrier dysfunction / photodamage / scarring / sensitised skin / mixed
- **Subtype** — hormonal vs comedonal acne, papulopustular vs erythematotelangiectatic rosacea, melasma vs PIH vs lentigines
- **Severity** — mild / moderate / severe (NICE-aligned where applicable)
- **Driver** — the underlying mechanism the plan needs to address

Classification is the bridge between examination and plan. **Without it, the plan is reactive. With it, the plan is reasoned.**

### 5. Setting expectations + cooling-off period

Before you write a single recommendation, you have the expectation conversation:

- **Realistic timelines** — most skin conditions need 8–12 weeks minimum to demonstrate response. Pigmentation needs 6+ months. Scarring needs 12+ months.
- **Likely vs unlikely outcomes** — what's achievable, what isn't, with the plan you're about to recommend
- **Maintenance vs cure** — most skin conditions are managed long-term, not cured
- **Risks of treatment** — barrier crash, post-inflammatory pigmentation, treatment-resistant flares
- **Cooling-off period** — minimum 24 hours for any first-time injectable; recommended for significant in-clinic plans

The cooling-off period is non-negotiable. **If a client makes a significant skin-treatment decision in the same visit they meet you, the consent is shakier than it should be.**

### 6. Consent + capacity

Every plan is documented with:

- Specific, written, version-dated consent for the treatments proposed
- Capacity considered (and noted) — particularly for clients on mental-health medication, those who appear distressed, or anyone where capacity is borderline
- Photography consent (separate from clinical consent) — covers progress tracking and any future marketing use
- Specific note on under-18s — in England, no cosmetic injectables under 18, full stop

This is the documentation that makes the plan defensible. Skip it and the plan is fragile from day one.

## What you'll notice

When you run this six-step flow on every consultation, three things change.

**Your plans hold longer.** Because the consultation surfaced the actual driver, the plan addressed the actual driver, and the client knew what to expect at every milestone.

**Your conversion rate goes up.** Counter-intuitively — the structured consultation feels more professional, the cooling-off period creates space for considered decision-making, and clients who say yes are clients who are committing properly.

**Your liability exposure drops.** The documentation file behind every plan is now defensible — to a GP query, an insurer review, a complaint, or a future regulator inspection.

This is what separates a skin therapist from a skin specialist. Not the techniques. The consultation.
