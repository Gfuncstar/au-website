Most skin plans don't fail because the practitioner is careless. They fail because the **consultation skipped the question that would have changed the plan**.

That's a strong claim. Let me prove it with three patterns I see every week.

## Pattern 1 — The acne plan that doesn't hold

A 32-year-old woman presents with persistent jawline acne. The practitioner identifies inflammatory acne, builds a sensible plan: gentle cleanser, BHA, retinoid, SPF. Twelve weeks later she's no better.

What got missed in the consultation? **The hormonal pattern.** Jawline-predominant acne in a 30-something woman is hormonal-pattern acne until proven otherwise. The plan needed to either include a referral conversation about hormonal pathway management, or set realistic expectations that topical-only would only do part of the job.

The plan wasn't wrong. The plan was **incomplete because the consultation was incomplete**.

## Pattern 2 — The rosacea plan that triggers a flare

A practitioner identifies papulopustular rosacea, prescribes a sensible barrier-supportive routine plus an anti-inflammatory active. Three weeks in, the client is worse — flushed, burning, irritated.

What got missed? **The barrier baseline.** The client's stratum corneum was already compromised — increased TEWL, reduced lipid content, sensitised to almost everything. The plan needed barrier repair *first*, anti-inflammatory *second*. Without that order, every active feels like an attack.

The plan wasn't wrong. **The order was wrong** — because the consultation didn't establish the barrier baseline before the plan was written.

## Pattern 3 — The pigmentation plan that creates more pigmentation

A client presents with what appears to be melasma. The practitioner builds a plan around hydroquinone alternatives — kojic acid, azelaic acid, niacinamide. Three months later, the pigmentation pattern looks the same and a new pattern has appeared along the temples and upper neck.

What got missed? **The trigger.** The client wears no sun protection, was never told photoprotection was the foundation of the entire plan, and was on no daily SPF routine. Topicals were lightening pigment from the bottom up while UV was creating new pigment from the top down faster than the actives could clear it.

The plan wasn't wrong. **The foundation was missing** — because the consultation didn't establish that photoprotection was the precondition, not an extra.

## The pattern in the patterns

Three different conditions. Three different plans. **One identical failure**: the consultation surfaced the visible problem but missed the underlying driver.

That's the consultation gap. And once you can see it, you can't un-see it — across acne, rosacea, pigmentation, barrier, photodamage, scarring, sensitised skin, and every condition you'll encounter.
