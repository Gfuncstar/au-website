Rosacea is a **chronic, inflammatory skin condition** primarily affecting the central face, characterised by persistent erythema, episodic flushing, visible blood vessels, inflammatory lesions, and heightened skin sensitivity. It is a **relapsing–remitting condition**, meaning symptoms fluctuate over time and can worsen without appropriate management.

Unlike acne vulgaris, rosacea is **not a disorder of comedone formation**. Instead, it is driven by a complex interaction between the **vascular system, innate immune response, nervous system, and skin barrier function**. These systems become dysregulated, leading to exaggerated inflammatory and vascular responses to otherwise normal internal or external stimuli.

## A neurovascular and immune-mediated disorder

Rosacea is increasingly understood as a **neurovascular and immune-mediated disorder**, rather than a purely cosmetic or inflammatory skin condition. Individuals with rosacea demonstrate heightened reactivity of facial blood vessels, abnormal immune signalling, and impaired barrier integrity, making the skin more vulnerable to triggers such as heat, UV exposure, stress, and topical products.

Clinically, rosacea presents along a **spectrum of phenotypes**, ranging from transient flushing and background redness to inflammatory papules, pustules, tissue thickening (phymatous change), and ocular involvement. Symptoms most commonly affect the cheeks, nose, chin, forehead, and periocular area, though extra-facial involvement can occur in advanced cases.

## Who develops rosacea

Rosacea typically develops in adulthood, often between the ages of 30 and 60, and is more frequently diagnosed in individuals with lighter skin phototypes — although it can affect all skin tones and is often **underdiagnosed in darker skin**. While more commonly reported in women, men may experience more severe or phymatous forms.

Importantly, rosacea carries a significant **psychosocial burden**, with many individuals experiencing reduced quality of life, embarrassment, anxiety, or social withdrawal due to visible symptoms and unpredictable flare-ups.

## There is no cure — but it can be stabilised

At present, **there is no cure for rosacea**. Management focuses on:

- Early identification
- Trigger recognition and avoidance
- Skin barrier repair
- Inflammation and vascular control
- Long-term maintenance

When appropriately managed, rosacea can be stabilised and significantly improved, preventing progression and reducing flare frequency.

## Why phenotype-led thinking matters

While these core principles underpin effective management, successful outcomes rely on a more detailed understanding of how rosacea presents in each individual.

Not all rosacea behaves the same, and treatment approaches must be tailored accordingly. Variations in subtype, trigger patterns, skin sensitivity, and underlying driver mechanisms all influence how the condition should be assessed and managed in practice.

For this reason, accurate identification of **rosacea phenotypes and contributing factors** is a critical next step. This allows practitioners to move beyond generic treatment plans and instead develop targeted, patient-specific strategies that improve both short- and long-term outcomes.
