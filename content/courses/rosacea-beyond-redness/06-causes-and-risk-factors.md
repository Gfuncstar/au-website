Rosacea has no single cause. It develops from a combination of biological predisposition and environmental triggers that, over time, push the skin's vascular, immune and barrier systems into a state of chronic over-reactivity.

That matters at the consultation chair. If you treat rosacea as a vascular problem only, you'll improve the redness and miss the immune driver. If you treat it as an immune problem only, you'll calm the inflammation and the next heatwave will undo your work. **You're treating a system, not a symptom** — and the seven drivers below are how that system fails.

## 1. Vascular reactivity

Repeated flushing trains the facial vasculature to dilate more readily and constrict less efficiently. Over time the vessels lose tone, the dilation becomes persistent erythema, and visible telangiectasia begins to form.

**Clinical relevance:** Each untreated flush episode contributes to long-term vascular damage. Stabilising vascular reactivity is foundational, not cosmetic — it changes the threshold for the next flare.

## 2. Neurogenic inflammation

Sensory nerves in rosacea-prone skin are hypersensitive. They release neuropeptides — substance P and CGRP among them — in response to heat, stress, exertion and emotional stimuli. Those neuropeptides drive vasodilation and recruit inflammatory mediators.

**Key point:** This is why rosacea reacts so quickly to stress and temperature change — it is partly a *neurogenic* condition, not purely a vascular one. Treat the nervous-system reactivity and you treat the trigger threshold.

## 3. Immune dysregulation

Rosacea-prone skin shows overactivation of the innate immune system. Toll-like receptor 2 (TLR-2) is over-expressed, which leads to excessive cleavage of cathelicidin into LL-37 fragments. Those fragments amplify vascular and inflammatory signalling — producing the flushing, swelling and tenderness that define a flare.

**Why this matters:** This is the molecular reason rosacea behaves as if the skin is overreacting to ordinary stimuli — because it is.

## 4. Barrier dysfunction

Increased transepidermal water loss, reduced ceramide content and impaired lipid composition leave the skin permeable to irritants, microbes and environmental triggers. A compromised barrier raises pH, lowers the threshold for inflammation, and lets every other driver hit harder.

**Clinical relevance:** Aggressive cleansing, exfoliation or active ingredients can deepen barrier impairment and worsen rosacea. The instinct to "do more" is the single most reliable way to make this condition worse.

## 5. Microbial imbalance

Increased density of *Demodex folliculorum*, altered commensal bacterial behaviour, and the resulting immune activation all contribute to inflammation. *Demodex* is present on most adult skin — what changes in rosacea is the density and the immune response to it.

**Important clarification:** Microbial dysbiosis is a contributor, not a primary cause. Treating it without addressing barrier and immune drivers won't hold the result.

## 6. Genetic predisposition

A family history of rosacea, easy flushing, or fair skin phototypes increases susceptibility. Genetics influence vascular reactivity, immune sensitivity and the threshold at which a trigger becomes a flare.

**Clinical relevance:** Genetic predisposition is the reason two clients with the same lifestyle and the same skincare can sit on completely different rosacea patterns. It also explains why management is long-term — you're working with the threshold, not against the genetics.

## 7. Lifestyle and environmental triggers

These don't cause rosacea. But for predisposed skin, they reliably push it into a flare:

- Heat (hot showers, hot rooms, hot drinks)
- Alcohol — red wine in particular
- Spicy food
- UV exposure
- Stress and strong emotion
- Extreme cold or wind
- Vigorous exercise
- Inappropriate skincare — acids, retinoids, fragrance, alcohol-based products

**Clinical tip:** Encourage clients to keep a flare diary for four to six weeks. Personal triggers are highly individual; pattern-spotting is one of the most effective long-term management tools you can teach. The diary itself often does more for client confidence than any in-clinic treatment in the same window.

## Putting it together

The seven drivers don't act in isolation. A typical flare is a **vascular event triggered by a neurogenic stimulus, amplified by immune over-reactivity, and prolonged by a compromised barrier** — modulated by genetics and pushed by lifestyle.

That sequence is why an assessment built around "what triggered today's redness?" is more useful than one built around "which product will reduce it?" — and it's why the management chapter that follows treats *foundation phase* as the first move, not the last.
