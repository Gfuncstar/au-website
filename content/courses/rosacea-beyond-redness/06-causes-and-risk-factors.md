Rosacea does not have a single cause. It develops from a combination of biological predisposition and environmental triggers that, over time, push the skin's vascular, immune and barrier systems into a state of chronic over-reactivity.

Understanding these contributing factors allows practitioners to identify why rosacea is flaring in each individual — and to design more targeted, ethical management plans.

## 1. Vascular reactivity

Repeated flushing trains the facial vasculature to dilate more readily and constrict less efficiently. Over time this produces persistent erythema and visible telangiectasia.

**Clinical relevance:** Each untreated flush episode contributes to long-term vascular damage. Stabilising vascular reactivity is foundational, not cosmetic.

## 2. Neurogenic inflammation

Sensory nerves in rosacea-prone skin are hypersensitive. They release neuropeptides such as substance P and CGRP in response to heat, stress and emotional stimuli, which in turn drive vasodilation and inflammation.

**Key point:** This is why rosacea reacts so quickly to stress, exertion and temperature change — it is partly a neurogenic condition, not just a vascular one.

## 3. Immune dysregulation

Rosacea-prone skin shows overactivation of the innate immune system, with increased Toll-like receptor (TLR-2) activity and overproduction of antimicrobial peptides (especially LL-37). The result is chronic inflammation in response to otherwise harmless stimuli.

## 4. Barrier dysfunction

Increased transepidermal water loss, reduced ceramide content and impaired lipid composition leave the skin permeable to irritants, microbes and environmental triggers.

**Clinical relevance:** Aggressive cleansing, exfoliation or active ingredients can worsen rosacea by deepening barrier impairment.

## 5. Microbial imbalance

Increased density of *Demodex folliculorum*, altered commensal bacterial behaviour and resulting immune activation all contribute to inflammation. Microbial dysbiosis is a contributor — not a primary cause.

## 6. Genetic predisposition

A family history of rosacea, flushing, or fair skin phototypes increases susceptibility. Genetics influence vascular reactivity, immune sensitivity and the threshold at which triggers cause symptoms.

## 7. Lifestyle and environmental triggers

While not direct causes, these factors reliably worsen rosacea:

- Heat and hot showers
- Alcohol (especially red wine)
- Spicy foods
- UV exposure
- Stress and strong emotion
- Extreme cold or wind
- Vigorous exercise
- Inappropriate skincare (acids, retinoids, fragrance, alcohol)

**Clinical tip:** Encourage clients to keep a flare diary. Personal triggers are highly individual, and pattern-spotting is one of the most effective long-term management tools you can teach.
