**Rosacea: when a "simple flush" becomes a clinical problem.**

Rosacea doesn't usually become complicated overnight. Complications develop slowly, as repeated inflammation, flushing, and barrier disruption begin to change the skin's structure and behaviour.

Think of complications as the **long-term cost of chronic inflammation**. A common misconception is that rosacea solely affects the skin. However, it can affect a multitude of systems. Let's explore this below.

## Skin structure & function, the skin stops protecting itself

- Barrier failure
- Extreme sensitivity
- Poor post-procedure recovery

**Aesthetic consequences:**

- Increased risk of irritation, burns, prolonged erythema
- Limited treatment tolerance
- Reduced results from active skincare

If the barrier fails, every treatment feels aggressive.

## Vascular system, blushing becomes permanent

**Complications:**

- Persistent erythema
- Telangiectasia
- Heat and exercise intolerance

**Aesthetic consequences:**

- IPL/laser often needed (but higher risk)
- Injectables may worsen redness
- Clients misdiagnosed as pigmentation cases

**Common mistakes:** Treating redness like melasma.

## Connective tissue & sebaceous glands, inflammation rewrites facial architecture

**Complications:**

- Tissue hypertrophy
- Fibrosis
- Sebaceous gland overgrowth

**Key manifestation:** Rhinophyma.

**Aesthetic implications:**

- Fillers can worsen distortion
- Requires early referral
- Often psychologically distressing

## Ocular system, the eyes are involved, even when the skin looks calm

**Complications:**

- Blepharitis
- Chronic dry eye
- Recurrent styes

**Aesthetic risk zones:**

- Lash lifts
- Extensions
- Peri-orbital heat-based devices

**Clinical relevance:** Ocular symptoms may precede facial signs. Always ask about eye discomfort during consultation, even when the skin looks calm.

## When to advise referral

Most mild cases can be managed with basic care (like lid hygiene and artificial tears), but there are clear points where referral is important:

- Persistent or worsening symptoms despite homecare
- Pain in the eye (not just irritation)
- Changes in vision (blurred, reduced, or fluctuating vision)
- Marked light sensitivity
- Signs of corneal involvement (this is more serious and needs urgent attention)
- Recurrent infections like repeated styes that don't settle

**Red flags:** In those cases, refer to a GP, optometrist, or ophthalmologist, especially because untreated ocular rosacea can, in more severe cases, affect the cornea and vision.
