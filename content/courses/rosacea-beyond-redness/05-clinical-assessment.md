Accurate clinical assessment is fundamental in the safe and effective management of rosacea. As aestheticians, your role is **not to medically diagnose**, but to recognise characteristic signs, identify patterns, and determine whether treatment is appropriate within your scope of practice.

Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory condition with variable presentation. A thorough consultation and structured skin assessment are essential to avoid misidentification and inappropriate treatment.

## 1. Client consultation and history taking

A comprehensive consultation should explore:

- Onset and duration of symptoms
- Frequency and severity of flare-ups
- Identifiable triggers (heat, alcohol, stress, spicy foods, skincare products)
- Current and previous skincare use
- Medical history and prescribed medications
- Family history of rosacea or inflammatory skin conditions

Rosacea commonly presents with a pattern of flushing that progresses to persistent erythema. Clients may report:

- Burning or stinging sensations
- Heightened sensitivity to products
- Sudden flare episodes triggered by environmental or emotional factors

Identifying patterns is key. Rosacea is often cyclical, and flare triggers are frequently individualised.

## 2. Visual and physical assessment

Clinical observation should assess:

- Persistent central facial erythema
- Telangiectasia (visible superficial blood vessels)
- Papules and pustules (without comedones)
- Skin texture and hydration levels
- Signs of barrier impairment
- Ocular involvement (red, irritated eyes, requires referral)

Good lighting and minimal stimulation are essential during assessment, as rosacea-prone skin may flush easily.

Differentiation is particularly important at Level 2–3. Rosacea must be distinguished from:

- Acne vulgaris (presence of comedones)
- Allergic or irritant contact dermatitis
- Seborrhoeic dermatitis
- Generalised sensitive or reactive skin

**Key point:** The absence of comedones is a key distinguishing feature between acne and papulopustular rosacea.

## 3. Recognising rosacea subtypes

While formal medical classification is outside the aesthetic scope, practitioners should recognise common clinical patterns:

- Persistent erythema with flushing
- Inflammatory papules and pustules
- Thickened or textural changes (advanced cases require referral)
- Ocular symptoms (must be referred)

Understanding presentation allows for safer treatment planning and appropriate referral where necessary.

## 4. Determining scope of practice

Safe practice requires recognising when referral is appropriate. Referral should be considered if:

- Symptoms are severe or rapidly worsening
- Ocular involvement is present
- There is significant skin thickening
- The client is using prescription medication
- Diagnosis is uncertain

Working collaboratively with medical professionals supports client safety and professional integrity.

## Rosacea consultation questionnaire

The full template lives in the course resources for download. Below is the structure to work through with each client presenting with rosacea-type concerns.

**Client details**, Name / Date / Practitioner

**Skin history**

- When did the redness or symptoms first start?
- Has it stayed the same / improved / worsened?
- Is the redness constant, or does it come and go?
- Have you been formally diagnosed with rosacea? Yes / No

**Flushing & triggers**

- Do you flush easily?
- What triggers your redness? (Heat / Alcohol / Spicy foods / Stress / Exercise / Skincare products / Other)
- How long does a flare-up usually last?
- Does your skin calm down on its own, or does it need intervention?

**Skin sensitivity**

- Does your skin experience stinging, burning, or itching?
- Do you react to water, touch, or temperature changes?
- Are there products you cannot tolerate?
- Is your skin always sensitive, or only sensitive during flare-ups?

**Current skincare routine**, Cleanser / Serums / Moisturiser / SPF

- How often do you exfoliate?
- Are you using active ingredients? (Acids / Retinoids / Vitamin C / None)
- Have any products made your skin worse?

**Barrier health indicators**

- Does your skin feel tight or dehydrated?
- Do you experience dryness or flaking?
- Does your skin feel better with minimal products?
- How does your skin feel after cleansing?

**Lifestyle factors**

- Stress levels: low / moderate / high
- Do you notice flare-ups during stress?
- How often are you exposed to sun or heat?
- Do certain foods or drinks trigger your skin?

**Medical & treatment history**

- Previous treatments: chemical peels / laser / IPL / other
- How did your skin respond?
- Are you using prescribed treatments?
- History of skin conditions or sensitivities

**Client awareness & goals**

- What do you think triggers your skin?
- What concerns you most about your skin?
- What results are you hoping to achieve?
