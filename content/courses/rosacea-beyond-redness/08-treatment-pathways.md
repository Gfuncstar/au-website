## Rosacea clinic management plan (NICE-aligned)

**Goal:** Stabilise the condition, avoid exacerbation, and work within scope.

## 1. Clinical assessment (every client)

Identify rosacea phenotype:

- Erythema / flushing
- Papules / pustules
- Ocular symptoms
- Phymatous changes

Then:

- Screen for red flags
- Document triggers and flare patterns
- Ask about facial steroid use

**Important:** If red flags are present, modify or defer treatment.

## 2. Foundation phase (non-negotiable)

Applies to all rosacea clients.

- Barrier repair focus
- Calming protocols only
- Education before intervention

Avoid:

- Heat
- Aggressive exfoliation
- "Corrective" treatments during active disease

## 3. In-clinic treatments (when stable only)

**Appropriate options:**

- Gentle calming facials (no heat or friction)
- Barrier-support treatments — e.g. niacinamide, low-strength azelaic acid — to reduce redness and inflammation and protect the barrier
- LED (non-thermal, low intensity)
- Cooling and lymphatic support techniques using very light pressure to reduce swelling and flushing

**Important:** Proceed with caution on IPL and vascular laser — these should be medical-led or shared care. Patch testing is essential, and intervals between treatments should be longer than usual.

## 4. Treatments to avoid or defer

- Heat-based skin tightening
- Medium-to-deep chemical peels
- Microneedling
- Aggressive actives
- Fillers in phymatous rosacea

**Key point:** If redness burns, do not chase it.

## 5. Collaborative care (NICE-aligned)

Encourage GP or dermatology input when:

- Inflammatory lesions are moderate to severe
- Topicals are required
- Oral medication may be needed
- Ocular or phymatous rosacea is present

## 6. Monitoring & review

Track:

- Post-treatment erythema duration
- Flare frequency
- Skin tolerance

Step back if redness lasts longer than baseline. Adjust the plan seasonally.

## Rosacea home treatment advice for your patient

### 1. Cleanse (AM & PM)

- Use a gentle, soap-free cleanser
- Lukewarm water only
- Pat dry — do not rub

Avoid: foaming cleansers, scrubs or cleansing brushes, and hot water.

### 2. Moisturise (AM & PM)

- Use a barrier-repair moisturiser
- Look for: ceramides, glycerin, panthenol, cholesterol

**Why this matters:** A repaired barrier reduces sensitivity and improves tolerance to every other treatment that follows.

### 3. Sun protection (every morning)

- SPF 30+ minimum (mineral preferred)
- Apply daily, even in winter
- Reapply if outdoors

**NICE emphasis:** UV exposure is a major rosacea trigger. NICE/CKS guidance treats daily photoprotection as a core part of management, not an optional add-on.

### 4. Trigger management

Encourage patients to identify personal triggers such as:

- Heat and hot showers
- Alcohol (especially red wine)
- Spicy foods
- Stress
- Extreme cold or wind

**Clinical tip:** Ask the client to keep a flare diary. Patterns reveal themselves over weeks, not days.

### 5. Products & behaviours to avoid

- Strong acids or exfoliants
- Retinoids (unless medically advised)
- Fragrance and alcohol-based products
- Trying lots of new products at once

**Best practice:** One change at a time. If the skin reacts, you'll know exactly what caused it.

### 6. Medical treatments (if prescribed)

Under GP or dermatology care, topical treatments may include:

- Metronidazole
- Azelaic acid
- Ivermectin

Use exactly as prescribed. Expect improvement over 6–12 weeks.

### 7. When to seek medical review

- Worsening redness or burning
- Eye discomfort or recurrent styes
- Thickening skin (especially the nose)
- Emotional distress linked to symptoms

**Key point:** The message to convey to clients — *calm skin first. Consistency matters more than intensity.*
