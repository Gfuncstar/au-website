## Case Study 1 — Mr. A, 58, restaurant owner (phymatous rosacea)

**Client profile**

- Age: 58
- Gender: Male
- Occupation: Restaurant owner
- Skin type: Fitzpatrick II
- Medical history: Mild hypertension (controlled), no known allergies

**Lifestyle factors**

- Regular alcohol consumption (red wine most evenings)
- High stress levels
- Frequent heat exposure (works in hot kitchen)
- Minimal SPF use

**Presenting concern**

Mr. A presents complaining of:

- Progressive thickening of the nose over the past 5–7 years
- Persistent facial redness
- Enlarged pores and uneven skin texture
- Occasional inflammatory papules
- Embarrassment and social self-consciousness

He reports that flushing episodes have occurred since his early 40s but were never treated.

**Clinical examination findings**

- Persistent centrofacial erythema
- Visible telangiectasia across cheeks and nose
- Nodular thickening of nasal tissue
- Irregular skin surface with sebaceous gland hypertrophy
- No active ocular involvement

The nasal tissue demonstrates **early-to-moderate rhinophyma**, associated with phymatous rosacea.

**Pathophysiology discussion**

Phymatous rosacea represents:

- Chronic inflammation
- Progressive dermal fibrosis
- Sebaceous gland hyperplasia
- Connective tissue remodelling
- Vascular dysregulation

Long-term untreated inflammation leads to **structural skin changes** rather than just superficial redness.

### Learning tasks

**Diagnosis — what subtype(s) of rosacea are present?**

- **Erythematotelangiectatic rosacea (ETR)** — evidenced by persistent centrofacial erythema and visible telangiectasia
- **Phymatous rosacea** — evidenced by tissue thickening and sebaceous gland hypertrophy of the nose (early-to-moderate rhinophyma)

**What clinical markers confirm phymatous involvement?**

- Nodular thickening of nasal tissue
- Irregular skin surface
- Enlarged pores
- Sebaceous gland hypertrophy
- Progressive structural change over several years

These indicate **dermal fibrosis and connective tissue remodelling**, not just superficial inflammation.

**Trigger identification — list at least five contributing triggers in this case:**

1. Regular alcohol consumption (vasodilation trigger)
2. Chronic heat exposure (kitchen environment)
3. High stress levels (neurovascular stimulation)
4. UV exposure (minimal SPF use)
5. Long-standing untreated inflammation
6. Possible dietary triggers
7. Occupational temperature fluctuations

**Treatment planning (aesthetic scope) — what treatments are safe?**

- Barrier-repair facials
- LED red light therapy (anti-inflammatory support)
- Gentle enzyme exfoliation (only if skin is stable and tolerant)
- Strict SPF education and compliance

**What treatments are contraindicated?**

- Aggressive chemical peels
- Medium/deep exfoliation
- Heat-based devices (RF, strong IPL in unstable skin)
- Microdermabrasion
- Strong manual stimulation
- Overuse of actives

Phymatous tissue is structurally altered and easily aggravated.

**Referral considerations — when should this client be referred to a GP or dermatologist?**

- Evidence of progressive tissue thickening
- Moderate to advanced rhinophyma
- Psychosocial distress
- Failure to respond to conservative management
- Suspicion of severe inflammatory activity

**Homecare recommendations**

Develop a barrier-supportive homecare plan focused on gentle cleansing, lipid-replenishing moisturisers, daily mineral SPF, and trigger education.

**Critical thinking question:** Why is early intervention in rosacea essential to prevent permanent skin remodelling changes?

## Case Study 2 — 34-year-old female (vascular / erythematotelangiectatic)

**Client profile**

- 34-year-old female
- Persistent cheek redness for 3 years
- Flushes with wine, heat, exercise
- Skin burns when applying "active" skincare
- No papules or pustules

**Assessment**

- Rosacea type: Vascular (erythematotelangiectatic)
- Red flags: Persistent erythema, burning sensation, trigger-related flares

### Student tasks

1. Would you treat today? Yes / No — why?
2. Which treatments should be avoided initially?
3. What phase of the treatment pathway is this client in?

### Best practice answer

- Do not treat aggressively
- Start with stabilisation and barrier repair
- Avoid heat, peels, microneedling
- Educate on triggers and daily SPF
- Consider referral discussion if redness is distressing

**Key point:** Redness that burns is inflammation, not a cosmetic problem.

## Case Study 3 — 29-year-old female (reactive / neurogenic)

**Client profile**

- 29-year-old female
- "Everything stings"
- Can't tolerate moisturiser or SPF
- No obvious papules
- Frequent post-treatment flares at other clinics

**Assessment**

- Rosacea type: Reactive / neurogenic
- Red flags: Extreme sensitivity, treatment-induced flares

### Student tasks

1. What is the primary treatment goal?
2. Should progression be fast or slow?
3. What is the biggest practitioner mistake here?

### Best practice answer

- Goal is **tolerance, not transformation**
- Minimalist skincare only
- No exfoliation or devices
- Biggest mistake: trying to "push through" sensitivity

**Key point:** In rosacea, **patience is a treatment**.
