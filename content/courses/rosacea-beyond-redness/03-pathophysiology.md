Rosacea is **not a surface-level skin issue**. Its visible symptoms are the end result of deep structural and functional changes involving the blood vessels, immune system, nervous system, sebaceous units, and skin barrier. Understanding these systems, and how they interact, is essential for safe and effective management.

## The facial vascular system

**The engine behind flushing and persistent redness.**

The central face contains a dense and superficial network of blood vessels, designed to regulate temperature and protect vital structures. In rosacea, this system becomes dysregulated.

**What goes wrong:**

- Blood vessels dilate too easily
- Vasoconstriction is impaired
- Repeated flushing episodes cause vessels to remain permanently enlarged

**Clinical outcome:**

- Persistent erythema
- Telangiectasia
- Heat, burning, and flushing sensations

**Key point:** Each untreated flush episode contributes to long-term vascular damage.

## Neurovascular regulation

**Why triggers feel immediate and intense.**

The skin is closely connected to the nervous system. In rosacea, sensory nerves are hypersensitive, amplifying normal stimuli.

**In rosacea:**

- Nerves release neuropeptides (e.g. substance P, CGRP)
- These signals trigger vasodilation and inflammation
- Stress and emotional responses directly affect skin symptoms

**Clinical outcome:**

- Sudden flushing
- Tingling, stinging, or burning
- Stress-induced flares

**Key point:** Rosacea is partly a **neurogenic inflammatory condition**, not just vascular.

## Innate immune system

**An immune response stuck on high alert.**

Rosacea skin shows overactivation of the innate immune system, particularly within the epidermis.

**Key changes include:**

- Increased Toll-like receptor (TLR-2) activity
- Overproduction of antimicrobial peptides (cathelicidins, especially LL-37)
- Heightened inflammatory signalling

**Clinical outcome:**

- Papules and pustules
- Chronic inflammation
- Delayed skin recovery

**Key point:** The immune system responds excessively to otherwise harmless stimuli.

## Skin barrier function

**Why rosacea skin reacts to everything.**

The stratum corneum acts as the skin's protective shield. In rosacea, this barrier is structurally and functionally impaired.

**Barrier dysfunction leads to:**

- Increased transepidermal water loss (TEWL)
- Reduced lipid content
- Greater penetration of irritants and microbes

**Clinical outcome:**

- Dryness and tightness
- Product intolerance
- Heightened sensitivity

**Key point:** Barrier repair is foundational, without it, inflammation persists.

## Sebaceous glands & pilosebaceous units

**Not acne, but still involved.**

Sebaceous glands are prominent in the central face, where rosacea commonly appears.

**In rosacea:**

- Sebaceous activity is altered (not blocked)
- Gland enlargement may occur in chronic disease
- Inflammation affects surrounding tissue

**Clinical outcome:**

- Enlarged pores
- Texture changes
- Contribution to phymatous rosacea

**Key point:** Rosacea is not driven by comedones, but sebaceous structures still play a role.

## Microbial interaction (Demodex)

**A trigger, not a cause.**

*Demodex* mites are a normal part of skin flora but may be present in higher density in rosacea.

**Their role:**

- Trigger immune activation
- Carry inflammatory bacteria
- Exacerbate papulopustular flares

**Clinical outcome:**

- Inflammatory lesions
- Treatment resistance in some clients

**Key point:** *Demodex* contributes to inflammation but does not cause rosacea on its own.
