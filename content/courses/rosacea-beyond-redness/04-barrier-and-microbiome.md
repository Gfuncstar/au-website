## The critical role of barrier integrity in rosacea

The epidermal barrier, primarily located within the stratum corneum, serves as both a physical and immunological defence system. Its primary functions are to regulate **transepidermal water loss (TEWL)** and protect the skin from environmental irritants, allergens, and microbial invasion.

In rosacea-prone skin, barrier dysfunction is frequently observed. Research indicates that individuals with rosacea may present with:

- Increased transepidermal water loss
- Altered lipid composition within the stratum corneum
- Reduced ceramide levels
- Heightened cutaneous sensitivity

When the barrier is compromised, permeability increases. This allows external triggers, such as UV radiation, temperature fluctuations, harsh skincare products, and pollution, to penetrate more easily. In turn, this can stimulate inflammatory pathways and contribute to vascular reactivity, resulting in erythema and flare episodes.

Barrier impairment may also increase **neurosensory stimulation**, which explains the common symptoms of burning, stinging, and discomfort reported by clients with rosacea.

For aesthetic practitioners, this reinforces the importance of prioritising barrier restoration before introducing corrective or advanced treatment modalities. Excessive exfoliation, high-strength active ingredients, and thermal stimulation may further compromise an already weakened barrier.

**Clinical relevance:** Clinical considerations should include selection of low-irritation, non-stripping cleansers; use of lipid-replenishing formulations containing **ceramides and essential fatty acids**; limiting mechanical and chemical exfoliation; consistent use of **broad-spectrum photoprotection**; and structured, gradual treatment progression.

Barrier repair enhances skin resilience, reduces inflammatory reactivity, and improves overall treatment tolerance.

## Microbiome balance

**The importance of microbiome balance in inflammation regulation.**

The skin microbiome consists of diverse microorganisms that exist in a symbiotic relationship with the host. This microbial ecosystem plays an essential role in **immune modulation, inflammation regulation, and maintenance of barrier homeostasis**.

Emerging evidence suggests that rosacea is associated with **microbial imbalance (dysbiosis)**. Observations include:

- Increased density of *Demodex folliculorum*
- Altered behaviour of commensal bacteria
- Enhanced immune system activation

Microbial dysbiosis may contribute to the activation of innate immune responses, leading to increased inflammatory mediator release. This can exacerbate erythema, papules, pustules, and overall skin sensitivity.

Disruption of the microbiome may also delay barrier recovery, perpetuating a cycle of inflammation and reactivity.

From a clinical perspective, overuse of harsh antimicrobials, excessive cleansing, and aggressive resurfacing treatments may further disturb microbial balance.

**Best practice:** At Level 2–3, principles include supporting barrier integrity to protect microbial diversity; selecting gentle, non-disruptive formulations; avoiding unnecessary antibacterial overcorrection; incorporating anti-inflammatory ingredients; and educating clients on consistent, minimalistic homecare routines.

Contemporary rosacea management recognises that **maintaining microbial equilibrium is more beneficial than attempting eradication**.

## Clinical significance

Barrier dysfunction and microbiome imbalance are interrelated and mutually reinforcing. Impairment of one system often negatively impacts the other, increasing susceptibility to inflammation and flare cycles.

An evidence-informed approach to rosacea management at Level 2–3 should therefore prioritise:

- Reduction of inflammatory triggers
- Restoration of epidermal barrier integrity
- Preservation of microbial balance
- Gradual, tolerance-based treatment planning

This structured approach improves clinical outcomes while ensuring client safety and comfort.
