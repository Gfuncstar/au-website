## Has it "come in" yet?

**Short answer:** It is not fully enacted in law yet — but it is not hypothetical.

The Traffic Light System in England is part of the government's agreed direction of travel, embedded in policy planning and guidance, and already being used as a risk-classification framework by regulators, insurers, councils, and professional bodies.

It is **pending formal implementation through licensing**, not an idea that may or may not happen.

## Where did the Traffic Light System come from?

The system originates from the UK Government's response to the **Keogh Review** and subsequent patient safety work around non-surgical cosmetic procedures.

It is now closely aligned with:

- Department of Health & Social Care (DHSC)
- Health and Care Act 2022 (England)
- Future practitioner and premises licensing
- Risk-based regulation principles used across healthcare

The Traffic Light System reflects how regulators already think — even before legislation is finalised.

## What is it aligned with?

The Traffic Light System is not owned by one body, but it aligns with and is actively referenced by:

**Joint Council for Cosmetic Practitioners (JCCP)**

- Uses risk-based categorisation
- Aligns competence, scope, and oversight to procedure risk
- Promotes proportional regulation

**Cosmetic Practice Standards Authority (CPSA)**

- Sets standards based on procedure risk
- Supports the same Green / Amber / Red logic

**MHRA (Medicines & Healthcare products Regulatory Agency)**

- Regulates products and devices
- Applies higher scrutiny to higher-risk procedures

**CQC (Care Quality Commission)**

- Already regulates Red-category procedures
- Uses a risk-escalation model consistent with the Traffic Light approach

**Local Authorities (Councils)**

- Already license and inspect based on risk to public safety
- Green and Amber procedures are already treated differently in practice

So while the licensing framework is pending, **the risk framework is active**.

## Why people think it has "already come in"

Because in practice:

- Councils already assess risk differently
- Insurers already restrict cover based on risk
- Advertising enforcement already escalates with risk
- CQC already enforces Red-level boundaries

The Traffic Light System is already shaping decisions, even without a single statutory document labelled "Traffic Light Law".

## Current status (England)

**Pending in law, active in practice.**

The **Health and Care Act 2022** gives government powers to introduce:

- Practitioner licensing
- Premises licensing
- Approved qualifications

The Traffic Light System is the framework expected to underpin that licensing.

Practices that already align with it will be **future-proofed**. Those that don't will have to retreat, retrain, or stop offering services later.

## Why this matters for practitioners now

This is the key educational message:

> The Traffic Light System is not about what you can get away with today. It is about what you will be expected to justify tomorrow.

Regulators assess:

- Risk
- Decision-making
- Defensibility

**Not popularity. Not confidence. Not "everyone else does it".**

## 1. Why the Traffic Light System exists

The Traffic Light System was not created to:

- Rank practitioners
- Create "levels" of success
- Encourage progression into higher-risk treatments

It exists to align four critical elements:

- Risk
- Practitioner competence
- Premises standards
- Oversight and accountability

The higher the risk, the higher the expectation.

This system is about **proportionate control, not ambition**.

## 2. The biggest mistake — misclassification

One of the most common regulatory failures in England is misclassifying treatments.

**Common mistakes:** Misclassification usually sounds like:

- "It's basically the same as…"
- "It's not really medical"
- "I only do a small amount"
- "I don't inject much"

**Risk does not reduce because you minimise it in language.**

If a treatment sits in Amber or Red territory, calling it green does not protect you.

## 3. If you can't justify it on paper, you can't defend it in practice

This is a core professional principle.

Every treatment decision should be defensible through:

- Documentation
- Scope of practice
- Insurance terms
- Licensing conditions
- Regulatory guidance

When something goes wrong, no one asks:

> "Did you mean well?"

They ask:

> "Why did you think this was appropriate — and where is the evidence?"
