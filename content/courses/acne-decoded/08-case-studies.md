## Case Study 1 — Teenage moderate acne (UK secondary school student)

**Client profile**

- Age: 15
- Gender: Male
- Location: UK
- Acne type: Moderate inflammatory acne
- Areas affected: Face and upper back

**Presentation**

The client presents with a combination of open and closed comedones, inflammatory papules and pustules. Acne has been present for approximately 18 months. He reports using harsh cleansers and scrubs daily in an attempt to "dry out" the skin.

**Key contributing factors**

- Puberty-related androgen increase
- Excess sebum production
- Over-cleansing and barrier disruption
- Poor understanding of acne management

**Professional considerations**

- High risk of worsening inflammation due to current skincare habits
- Potential for scarring if inflammation continues
- Emotional sensitivity due to age and peer pressure

**Management approach**

- Education on acne as an inflammatory condition
- Gentle cleansing routine
- Introduction of appropriate home care actives
- Avoidance of aggressive exfoliation
- Monitor response over 8–12 weeks

**Referral consideration:** Refer to GP if no improvement after 12 weeks or if scarring develops.

**Applied learning questions**

1. Which of the four pathophysiological drivers are most active in this case?
2. Why might over-cleansing worsen acne in this client?
3. What education strategies are important when working with adolescents?

## Case Study 2 — Adult hormonal acne (UK working professional)

**Client profile**

- Age: 34
- Gender: Female
- Occupation: Office-based professional
- Acne type: Adult hormonal acne

**Presentation**

Persistent inflammatory acne along the jawline and chin, worsening pre-menstrually. Acne began in late twenties. The client reports high stress levels and frequent product changes influenced by social media.

**Key contributing factors**

- Hormonal fluctuations
- Stress-related cortisol elevation
- Inconsistent skincare routines
- Possible barrier impairment

**Professional considerations**

- Adult acne is often chronic and relapsing
- High emotional impact despite "moderate" severity
- Risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation

**Management approach**

- Stabilise skincare routine
- Introduce anti-inflammatory home care
- Support barrier repair
- Set realistic timelines
- Discuss long-term management rather than "cure"

**Referral consideration:** GP referral if acne persists, worsens, or hormonal imbalance is suspected.

**Applied learning questions**

1. Why is this acne pattern considered hormonal?
2. How does stress influence acne pathophysiology?
3. How would you manage expectations in this client?
