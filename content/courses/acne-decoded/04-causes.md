Acne develops as a result of multiple interacting causes, rather than a single trigger. Understanding these causes allows practitioners to identify why acne is occurring in each individual and to design more effective, ethical treatment plans.

This module focuses on the core biological causes of acne, alongside key influencing factors.

![seven interlocking causes, hormones drive, six others amplify.](/illustrations/acne-decoded/04-causes/01-dna-helix.png)

## 1. Hormonal influence (primary cause)

Hormones play a central role in acne development, particularly androgens.

- Androgens stimulate sebaceous glands to enlarge
- Increased androgen activity leads to excess sebum production
- Hormonal fluctuations occur during:
  - Puberty
  - Menstrual cycles
  - Pregnancy
  - Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)
  - Perimenopause and menopause

**Clinical relevance:** Hormonal acne often presents on the jawline, chin, and neck, tends to be inflammatory, and may be persistent or cyclical.

![Three-step cascade from rising androgens through sebaceous gland enlargement to sebum overproduction, with the five most common hormonal triggers, puberty, menstrual cycle, PCOS, pregnancy, perimenopause, surfaced underneath.](/illustrations/acne-decoded/04-causes/02-hormonal-pathway.svg)

## 2. Excess sebum production

Sebum is essential for skin protection, but excessive production creates an environment prone to acne.

- Excess oil mixes with dead skin cells
- Follicular flow becomes sluggish
- Pores are more likely to block
- Sebum-rich follicles favour bacterial overgrowth

**Key point:** Oily skin does not automatically mean acne, but excess sebum is a necessary contributor to acne formation.

## 3. Abnormal skin cell shedding (hyperkeratinisation)

In acne-prone skin, keratinocytes do not shed normally.

- Dead skin cells become sticky
- Cells clump together inside the follicle
- A blockage (microcomedone) forms

This is often the earliest stage of acne, occurring before visible lesions appear.

**Clinical relevance:** This explains why acne can feel "under the skin" and why prevention-focused treatment is essential.

## 4. *Cutibacterium acnes* imbalance

*Cutibacterium acnes* is part of the normal skin microbiome.

In acne-prone follicles:

- Blocked pores create a low-oxygen environment
- *C. acnes* multiplies excessively
- Bacterial by-products trigger inflammation

**Important clarification:** Acne is not an infection, it is an inflammatory response to bacterial imbalance.

## 5. Inflammation (central cause)

Inflammation is now recognised as a primary driver of acne, not just a secondary effect.

- Inflammatory mediators are present before lesions form
- Follicular rupture releases contents into surrounding tissue
- Immune activation leads to redness, swelling, and pain

**Clinical relevance:** Aggressive or stripping treatments often worsen acne by increasing inflammation.

## 6. Genetic predisposition

Genetics influence:

- Sebum production levels
- Inflammatory response intensity
- Hormonal sensitivity
- Scarring risk

Clients with a family history of acne may develop acne earlier, and experience more severe or persistent acne.

## 7. Lifestyle and environmental contributors

While not direct causes, these factors can trigger or worsen acne:

- Chronic stress (cortisol increases sebum and inflammation)
- Occlusion and friction (e.g. masks, helmets)
- Urban pollution
- Inappropriate skincare use
- Over-exfoliation or barrier damage
