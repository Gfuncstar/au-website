Based on **NICE guidance for acne vulgaris**.

## 1. Confirm it is acne vulgaris

Before grading severity, confirm the diagnosis.

Acne vulgaris is characterised by:

- Comedones (open or closed)
- Inflammatory lesions (papules, pustules)
- ± Nodules or cysts
- Typical distribution: face, chest, back

**Red flags / not typical acne (refer or reassess):**

- Sudden onset, severe, or late-onset acne
- Predominantly nodular acne in women (consider hormonal causes)
- Acne with virilisation, menstrual irregularities
- Rosacea, folliculitis, perioral dermatitis mimicking acne

## 2. Identify lesion types

| Lesion type | Description | Inflammatory | Clinical significance |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|-----------------------|
| Open comedone (blackhead) | Dilated follicle with dark surface plug | No | Indicates comedonal acne |
| Closed comedone (whitehead) | Small, skin-coloured or white bump caused by blocked follicle | No | Common in mild acne |
| Papule | Red, raised lesion with no visible pus | Yes | Indicates inflammatory acne |
| Pustule | Raised lesion containing visible pus with surrounding erythema | Yes | Active inflammation present |
| Nodule / cyst | Large, deep, painful lesion; firm or fluctuant on palpation | Yes (severe) | High risk of scarring; requires escalation |

## 3. Assess acne severity (NICE categories)

**Mild Acne**
- Mostly comedones
- Few papules or pustules
- No nodules
- Minimal scarring risk

*Typical presentation:* Teen or adult with blackheads and occasional spots.

**Moderate Acne**
- More widespread papules and pustules
- Inflammatory lesions present
- Limited nodules may appear
- Trunk may be involved

*Typical presentation:* Persistent acne not responding to basic OTC treatments.

**Severe Acne**
- Nodules and/or cysts
- Extensive inflammation
- High risk of scarring
- Significant psychological impact

*Typical presentation:* Painful acne, deep lesions, visible scarring or pigment changes.

## 4. Map the distribution

Document where acne appears — this influences treatment and referral.

Check:

- Face (T-zone vs jawline)
- Chest
- Upper back / shoulders

**Clinical tip:** Jawline-predominant acne in adult women may suggest hormonal influence.

## 5. Assess scarring & post-inflammatory changes

Look for:

- Atrophic scars (ice-pick, boxcar, rolling)
- Hypertrophic or keloid scars
- Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)
- Post-inflammatory erythema (PIE)

**NICE emphasis:** Scarring risk alone justifies escalation of treatment, even if acne appears "mild".

## 6. Assess psychosocial impact (core NICE guidance)

Ask directly:

- "How does your skin affect your confidence?"
- "Do you avoid social situations because of your acne?"
- "Is it affecting work, school, or relationships?"

**Immediate referral if:**

- Severe distress
- Anxiety or depression linked to acne
- Body image concerns or low self-esteem

## 7. Take a targeted acne history

Cover:

- Duration of acne
- Previous treatments (OTC, prescriptions, isotretinoin)
- Skincare routine & product use
- Diet perceptions (without reinforcing myths)
- Menstrual cycle / hormonal contraception (if relevant)
- Family history of severe acne or scarring

## 8. Document and photograph

- Use consistent lighting
- Same angles each visit
- Neutral facial expression
- Consent documented

**Photos are essential for:** monitoring response, justifying escalation, managing patient expectations.
