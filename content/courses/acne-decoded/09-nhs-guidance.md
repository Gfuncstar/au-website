In the UK, acne management follows national clinical guidance produced by **NICE**. Understanding this guidance is essential for safe, ethical, and effective acne treatment, particularly when working with clients who may be at risk of scarring or emotional distress.

![NICE-aligned, defensible practice, built on national clinical guidance.](/illustrations/acne-decoded/09-nhs-guidance/01-document.png)

## 1. Why NICE guidance matters

NICE guidance:

- Sets the national standard for acne care
- Is used by GPs, dermatologists, and NHS services
- Emphasises evidence-based treatment and patient safety

For skin professionals, aligning with NICE:

- Protects scope of practice
- Improves referral decisions
- Enhances professional credibility

## 2. Severity is more than what you see

One of the most important NICE principles is that **acne severity is not judged by appearance alone**.

NICE considers:

- Lesion type and distribution
- Duration of acne
- Presence of scarring
- Psychological impact

This means:

- "Moderate-looking" acne may still require escalation
- Emotional distress alone can justify referral

## 3. Acne is treated as a chronic condition

NICE does not view acne as something to "clear quickly".

Instead:

- Improvement is expected over weeks to months
- Treatment plans should be reviewed at 12 weeks
- Maintenance is often required to prevent relapse

This reinforces the importance of client education and expectation management.

## 4. Treatment escalation and referral

NICE guidance supports referral when:

- Acne is severe
- Scarring risk is present
- Treatment has failed
- Mental wellbeing is affected

Referral is not a failure, it is a professional responsibility.

## 5. Psychological impact is clinically relevant

NICE explicitly recognises:

- Acne's impact on confidence and quality of life
- Emotional distress as a factor in severity
- The need for compassionate communication

This means professionals must assess **how acne affects the person, not just the skin**.

## 6. Applying NICE guidance in practice

Professionals should:

- Work within scope
- Support, not replace, medical care
- Refer early when red flags appear
- Use NICE principles to guide decision-making

Aligning with NICE improves client safety, treatment outcomes, and professional integrity.
