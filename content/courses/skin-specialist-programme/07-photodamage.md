The anti-ageing market is the loudest in cosmetic skin care and the one most full of marketing. This module separates **what the evidence supports** from what's brand-funded research, and gives you a hierarchy of what to recommend.

## What this module covers

- Intrinsic vs extrinsic ageing biology
- Sun-damage assessment (Glogau, Fitzpatrick)
- The anti-ageing treatment hierarchy
- Retinoids, vitamin C, niacinamide — evidence + sequencing
- Photoprotection as the foundation
- In-clinic adjuncts and realistic outcomes

## 1. Intrinsic vs extrinsic ageing

Two completely different processes happen at the same time, and they need different responses.

**Intrinsic ageing** — the genetically programmed slow decline of skin function. Fixed rate. Affects everyone. Manifests as:

- Reduced cell turnover (slows from ~28 days at 20yo to ~40+ days at 60yo)
- Reduced collagen synthesis (~1% loss per year from 30+)
- Reduced elastin function (recoil decreases)
- Reduced sebaceous activity
- Reduced barrier lipid production
- Vascular thinning
- Pigmentation drift (lentigines accumulate)

**Treatment effect:** Modest. You can support it but you can't reverse it.

**Extrinsic ageing** — environmental damage layered on top of intrinsic. ~80% of visible "ageing" is extrinsic. Driven by:

- **UV radiation** (the dominant driver — ~70% of visible ageing)
- **Visible light + infrared** (especially for pigmentation)
- **Pollution** (oxidative stress)
- **Smoking** (significant collagen + microvascular impact)
- **Diet / nutrient status** (antioxidants, omega-3, glycation)
- **Sleep + stress** (cortisol, repair cycles)

**Treatment effect:** Significant. Extrinsic damage is the part you can actually treat.

**Clinical principle:** Most "anti-ageing" treatments work primarily on extrinsic damage, not intrinsic. Set expectations accordingly. A 60-year-old client can look like a less-photodamaged 60-year-old. They cannot look 40.

## 2. Sun-damage assessment

Two complementary assessment frameworks.

**Glogau classification** — categorises photodamage:

- **Type I (mild)** — no keratoses, little wrinkling, minimal pigmentary change. Late 20s / 30s.
- **Type II (moderate)** — early actinic keratoses, parallel smile lines forming, early pigmentary change. 30s / 40s.
- **Type III (advanced)** — visible keratoses, persistent wrinkles at rest, dyschromia, telangiectasia. 50s / 60s.
- **Type IV (severe)** — actinic keratoses, deep wrinkles throughout, severe dyschromia, possible skin cancer. 60s+.

**Fitzpatrick I–VI** — informs treatment selection (UV damage risk vs PIH risk).

**Visual inspection clues for photodamage:**

- Comparison: dorsal forearm vs underarm (sun-protected vs sun-exposed) — striking
- Solar elastosis (yellow, leathery texture)
- Lentigines (sun spots) accumulation
- Telangiectasia on cheeks and nose
- Actinic keratoses (rough, scaly, often at hairline / temples / dorsal hands)

**Document the Glogau type in the consultation.** It anchors realistic outcome expectations.

## 3. The anti-ageing treatment hierarchy

Ranked by evidence + impact:

**Tier 1 — non-negotiable foundation:**

- **Daily broad-spectrum SPF** — the single highest-impact intervention. Period.
- **Sun avoidance behaviours** — peak UV avoidance, hat / sunglasses
- **Smoking cessation** — significant impact if applicable

**Tier 2 — strong evidence, broad applicability:**

- **Topical retinoids** (tretinoin, adapalene, retinol) — only topical with consistent evidence for collagen synthesis and photoageing reversal
- **Topical vitamin C** (L-ascorbic acid 10–20%) — antioxidant, photoprotection support, mild collagen support
- **Topical niacinamide** (5–10%) — barrier support, mild pigmentation, sebum modulation
- **Antioxidant supplementation** — controversial but pragmatically useful (vitamin C + E, polyphenols)

**Tier 3 — moderate evidence, condition-specific:**

- **Peptides** (matrixyl, copper peptides) — modest evidence, marketing-heavy
- **Growth factors** (EGF) — emerging evidence
- **Glycolic acid / chemical peels** — supportive, especially for texture and pigmentation
- **Oral collagen supplements** — emerging evidence, modest effect

**Tier 4 — in-clinic procedural:**

- **Microneedling** — collagen induction therapy, evidence-based for texture, scars
- **Chemical peels** (medium depth) — for established photodamage in appropriate skin types
- **Light-based treatments** (IPL, lasers) — evidence-based for telangiectasia, lentigines, surface texture
- **Radiofrequency / HIFU** — emerging evidence for skin tightening
- **Injectables** — outside skin-specialist scope; refer

**Recommend in this hierarchy.** A client with no SPF habit doesn't need a £400 vitamin C serum. They need SPF.

## 4. Retinoids — the workhorse

The single most evidence-based topical for photoageing.

**The mechanism:**

- Modulates gene expression in keratinocytes
- Accelerates cell turnover (compensates for the intrinsic slowdown)
- Stimulates collagen synthesis
- Reduces matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) activity that breaks down collagen
- Modulates pigmentation via melanocyte signalling

**Forms (decreasing potency):**

- **Tretinoin** (prescription) — highest evidence, most-irritating
- **Adapalene** (over-the-counter in some jurisdictions) — good evidence, better tolerance
- **Retinaldehyde** — moderate evidence, good tolerance
- **Retinol** — moderate evidence, mild action
- **Retinyl esters** (palmitate, propionate) — minimal action

**Sequencing rules:**

- Start at lowest concentration
- Once or twice weekly for 2 weeks
- Build to every other night over 4-8 weeks
- Pair with barrier support always
- Never combine with AHA in same regimen unless very experienced
- Pregnancy: avoid topical tretinoin / retinol; azelaic acid is safe alternative

## 5. Photoprotection as the foundation

If a client only does ONE thing, it's daily broad-spectrum SPF.

**Best practice for photoageing prevention:**

- SPF 30–50 broad-spectrum, daily, year-round
- Apply 2 mg/cm² (most people apply 1/4 of this)
- Reapply every 2 hours when exposed
- Mineral or hybrid filters preferred for melasma-prone clients
- Tinted SPF protects against visible light (additional benefit for pigmentation)

**Frame it as treatment, not maintenance.** A client who understands SPF is the foundation of every anti-ageing plan follows it. A client who hears "use sunscreen" as advice doesn't.

## 6. In-clinic adjuncts

Layered onto a strong topical foundation:

**Microneedling** — evidence-based for texture, fine lines, mild-moderate photodamage. Multi-session protocol typical (3-6 sessions).

**Chemical peels** — superficial (mandelic, lactic) for support; medium-depth (TCA) for established photodamage in Fitzpatrick I-III.

**Light-based:**

- **IPL** — telangiectasia, lentigines (in pale skin)
- **Vascular lasers** (KTP, PDL) — telangiectasia
- **Pigment lasers** (Q-switched, picosecond) — lentigines, post-procedure pigmentation
- **Fractional lasers** (non-ablative, ablative) — established photodamage; specialist territory

**Injectables** — outside skin-specialist scope; this programme doesn't teach injection. Refer to qualified injector for filler / toxin discussions.
