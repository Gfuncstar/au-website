The Skin Specialist consultation is the **single most important asset** you'll build in this programme. Every condition module that follows assumes this framework is running underneath the plan. **Without it, the plans are protocols. With it, the plans are reasoning.**

The Mini course introduced the six-step shape. This module is the deeper version — what each step is actually for, the questions inside each step, and the documentation that makes the whole thing defensible.

## The six steps, deeper

### Step 1 — Pre-consultation intake

The intake form is a clinical asset, not an admin tool. Treat it like one.

A defensible intake captures:

- **Demographic data** — age, hormonal stage, pregnancy / breastfeeding status, medications (with doses where known), allergies
- **Concern-in-their-words** — open-ended box, no leading questions
- **Goal hierarchy** — what's the primary concern, what's secondary, what's tertiary. Forces ranking.
- **Previous treatments** — OTC, prescribed, in-clinic. With approximate dates and outcomes.
- **Photographic baseline** — submitted before the visit where possible, or taken in standardised lighting on arrival
- **Skin-type self-report** — useful for orientation, never as diagnosis
- **Lifestyle factors** — sun exposure, smoking, alcohol, sleep, stress (broad strokes; deeper if relevant to the plan)

**Why this matters:** A 20-minute consultation that starts with no intake gets through about 40% of what a 20-minute consultation that starts with full intake gets through. The intake form **buys you time** to do the clinical thinking that turns visible problems into diagnosed drivers.

### Step 2 — Structured history

Every consultation, the same questions, the same order:

**Onset** — when did this start? Sudden vs gradual onset is the first divergence in differential diagnosis.

**Duration** — how long has the current pattern been present? Acute (< 6 weeks) vs subacute (6 weeks – 6 months) vs chronic (> 6 months) changes the management approach significantly.

**Pattern over time** — better, worse, cyclical (e.g. premenstrual flare), stable. Cyclical patterns point to hormonal involvement. Worsening patterns suggest progression. Stable patterns are often defensive plateaus.

**Triggers** — open question first ("does anything make it worse?"), then specific prompts:
- Sun / heat / cold
- Stress
- Alcohol / spicy food
- Skincare products
- Hormonal cycle
- Sleep / fatigue
- Medications (including OTC)

**Previous treatments** — what's been tried, in what order, with what outcome. Particularly important: which treatments **made it worse**? That's diagnostic.

**Medical history** — current and past. Particularly relevant: thyroid disease (skin involvement), autoimmune conditions, hormonal conditions (PCOS, endometriosis, thyroid), recent steroid use, isotretinoin history, immunosuppression.

**Family history** — same condition or related (atopy triad: eczema/asthma/hay fever; pigmentation tendencies; rosacea; acne severity).

**Why this matters:** Each question has a specific clinical job. Skip one and you miss a layer. The discipline isn't memorising the questions — it's running them in the same order every time so you don't omit by accident.

### Step 3 — Structured examination

Distribution, lesion type, skin tone, barrier baseline.

**Distribution mapping:**

- **Central face** — common site for rosacea (cheeks, nose, chin, central forehead)
- **T-zone** — common site for adolescent acne, sebaceous prominence
- **Jawline / chin / neck** — adult hormonal acne pattern
- **Periorbital** — sensitive area, common site for sensitised skin and dermatitis
- **Trunk** — chest / shoulders / back as adolescent acne distribution; differential considerations for adult acne on trunk
- **Sun-exposed areas** — temples, dorsal hands, lower legs for photodamage and lentigines

**Lesion-type classification:**

- Open vs closed comedones (acne, comedonal)
- Inflammatory papules and pustules (acne / rosacea / folliculitis differential)
- Telangiectasia (rosacea, photodamage, post-procedure)
- Erythema (rosacea, sensitised, contact dermatitis)
- Scaling / plaques (eczema, psoriasis, seborrheic dermatitis)
- Pigmented patches (melasma, PIH, lentigines)
- Atrophic / hypertrophic scars

**Skin-tone classification (Fitzpatrick I–VI):**

Affects every plan. Particularly:

- Fitzpatrick I–II — high UV damage risk, lower PIH risk
- Fitzpatrick III–IV — moderate UV risk, moderate PIH risk
- Fitzpatrick V–VI — lower UV damage risk, **high PIH risk** (changes treatment selection significantly)

**Barrier baseline:**

Even without a TEWL meter, a competent visual assessment captures:

- Visible erythema (background reactive skin)
- Dehydration lines (fine creping, dullness)
- Scaling / flaking (compromised stratum corneum)
- Tactile sensitivity (does the patient flinch when you touch?)
- Recent over-treatment (multiple current actives, recent peels, recent in-clinic procedures)

**Photographic documentation:**

- Same lighting (consistent setup, ideally cross-polarised for vascular and pigmentation)
- Same angles (front, left 45°, right 45°, side profile both sides)
- Same expression (neutral)
- Same distance from camera
- Dated, labelled, stored on the clinical record

This isn't a vanity exercise. It's the clinical record that proves treatment response and protects you in a complaint.

### Step 4 — Classification

You name what you're looking at:

- **Condition** — primary and secondary if both present
- **Subtype** — particularly important for rosacea (4 subtypes), acne (comedonal vs inflammatory vs nodular), pigmentation (melasma vs PIH vs lentigines)
- **Severity** — mild / moderate / severe (NICE-aligned where applicable)
- **Driver** — the underlying mechanism the plan needs to address (hormonal, inflammatory, photoageing, barrier-failure, etc.)

Classification is the **bridge between examination and plan**. A good classification statement reads like:

> "Moderate inflammatory acne with hormonal-pattern distribution along the jawline and chin, in a 32-year-old female, Fitzpatrick III, with mild barrier compromise from over-treatment with multiple actives in the last 6 months."

That's not paperwork. That's the **plan written in advance** — every word in the classification points to a treatment choice.

### Step 5 — Setting expectations + cooling-off period

**Realistic timelines** are non-negotiable in the consultation. Most plans fail not because the plan was wrong, but because the client expected results in three weeks and the condition needs three months.

Standard expectation framing:

- **Acne** — 8–12 weeks for meaningful response, 6 months for full optimisation
- **Rosacea** — 12 weeks for inflammatory subtypes, 6+ months for vascular changes (telangiectasia rarely fully resolves with topicals alone)
- **Pigmentation** — 6+ months for melasma, longer for stubborn PIH, photoprotection lifelong
- **Photodamage** — 6+ months for visible improvement, ongoing for maintenance
- **Scarring** — 12+ months minimum, often years for full optimisation

**Likely vs unlikely outcomes:**

- "Likely" outcomes are what the evidence supports for this client given their condition, severity, and adherence
- "Unlikely" outcomes are what the client is hoping for that the plan probably can't deliver
- Both go in the documentation. Both go into the conversation.

**Cooling-off period:**

- Minimum 24 hours for any first-time injectable
- Recommended 48 hours for significant in-clinic plans (chemical peels, microneedling, laser/IPL)
- The cooling-off period is in the documentation, even if the client wants to proceed same day

**Why this matters:** Cooling-off is consent quality. A client making a significant cosmetic decision in the same visit they meet you has consent that's harder to defend. The cooling-off creates space for considered consent.

### Step 6 — Consent + capacity

**Specific written consent** for every treatment in the plan. Generic "I consent to treatment" is not enough. The form names:

- Each treatment, by name
- The risks (most likely and most serious)
- The expected outcome
- The alternative options (including no treatment)
- The cost
- The cancellation / refund policy

**Capacity assessment:**

For most clients, capacity is straightforward and noted as such. **For some, it is not** — and the documentation should reflect a considered assessment:

- Clients on mental-health medication where mood / capacity may fluctuate
- Clients who appear distressed about their appearance
- Clients with declared body dysmorphic features (BDD risk)
- Clients with significant cognitive impairment

In any of these cases, capacity is documented explicitly, and a reflective note explains why you considered them able to consent.

**Photography consent** is separate from clinical consent and covers progress tracking, future use in marketing (if any), and storage period.

**Under-18 specific note:** No cosmetic injectables under 18 in England. Ever. The Botulinum Toxin and Cosmetic Fillers (Children) Act 2021 makes this a legal prohibition, not a clinical preference.

## Documentation as a defensibility asset

Every consultation produces a clinical record that contains:

- Intake form data
- History notes (structured)
- Examination findings (structured)
- Photographic baseline
- Classification statement
- Plan with timelines
- Consent forms (signed, dated, version-controlled)
- Capacity note where appropriate

**This file is the difference between a defensible practice and an indefensible one.** When a complaint, an insurer review, a CQC inspection, or a regulatory enquiry arrives, the clinical record is the answer.
