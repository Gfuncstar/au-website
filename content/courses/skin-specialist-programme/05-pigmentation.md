Pigmentation disorders are the conditions most often **made worse by the wrong plan**. Aggressive treatment of melasma in Fitzpatrick IV–VI can drive PIH that's harder to treat than the original problem. This module is about treating pigmentation in a way that respects the biology and the skin tone.

## What this module covers

- Melanin biology and Fitzpatrick assessment
- Differentiating melasma vs PIH vs lentigines
- Hydroquinone vs alternatives (kojic, azelaic, tranexamic)
- Photoprotection as the foundation, not an extra
- Skin-of-colour considerations
- Risk of rebound and ethical practice

## 1. Melanin biology

Pigment forms when melanocytes produce melanin in response to a trigger and transfer melanosomes to surrounding keratinocytes. The triggers are almost always one of:

- **UV radiation** (UVA and UVB)
- **Inflammation** (post-acne, post-procedure, eczema, contact dermatitis)
- **Hormones** (oestrogen, progesterone, particularly in pregnancy and oral contraceptive use)
- **Heat** (visible light + infrared, often overlooked in melasma)

**Clinical relevance:** Treatment that doesn't address the trigger doesn't hold. You can lighten melasma with topicals; if the client doesn't wear SPF, the melasma comes back, often worse.

## 2. Differentiating melasma vs PIH vs lentigines

Three different conditions, three different management approaches. Misdiagnosis here drives a lot of treatment failure.

**Melasma**

- **Distribution**, symmetric, central face (cheeks, forehead, upper lip, sometimes mandible)
- **Colour**, light to dark brown, sometimes greyish in deeper depths
- **Trigger**, hormonal + UV; very common in pregnancy and OCP use
- **Behaviour**, chronic, often relapsing, photo-aggravated
- **Demographics**, most common in women, Fitzpatrick III–V
- **Wood's lamp**, accentuated where pigment is epidermal, less so where dermal

**Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)**

- **Distribution**, wherever inflammation occurred (post-acne lesions, post-procedure, post-eczema)
- **Colour**, brown to dark brown, sometimes black in deeper skin tones
- **Trigger**, preceding inflammation
- **Behaviour**, fades with time (months to years), accelerated by topicals + photoprotection
- **Demographics**, common in Fitzpatrick III–VI

**Lentigines (sun spots)**

- **Distribution**, sun-exposed areas (face, dorsal hands, forearms, chest)
- **Colour**, well-defined brown, usually homogeneous
- **Trigger**, cumulative UV exposure
- **Behaviour**, stable, slowly accumulates with age
- **Demographics**, Fitzpatrick I–III most common, but possible in any skin tone

**Why differentiation matters:** Melasma needs gentler, longer-term, hormonal-aware treatment. PIH needs photoprotection + tyrosinase inhibitors and time. Lentigines often respond well to in-clinic light-based treatments. Same approach to all three doesn't work.

## 3. The treatment ladder

**Foundation, every pigmentation plan, no exceptions:**

- **Photoprotection**, broad-spectrum SPF 50, mineral or hybrid preferred for melasma (visible light protection matters), reapplied every 2 hours, every day
- **Trigger management**, heat avoidance for melasma, UV avoidance for all
- **Hormonal review**, discuss OCP / HRT for melasma if relevant; often improves with cessation but takes months

**Tyrosinase inhibitors (the topical workhorses):**

- **Azelaic acid 15–20%**, gentle, multi-mechanism, well-tolerated in pregnancy, good first-line
- **Niacinamide 5–10%**, supportive, anti-inflammatory, well-tolerated
- **Tranexamic acid (topical, 2–5%)**, useful for melasma specifically; emerging evidence
- **Kojic acid**, moderate effect, can be irritating
- **Vitamin C**, antioxidant + tyrosinase modulator; sequence carefully (morning + SPF)
- **Retinoids**, accelerate cell turnover; introduce slowly to avoid PIH worsening from irritation

**Hydroquinone, the controversial workhorse:**

- Effective at 2–4% short-term
- Banned over-the-counter in the UK; available on prescription
- **Risk of ochronosis** with prolonged use (paradoxical worsening in deeper skin tones)
- Best used as a short course (8–12 weeks) under prescription, then transitioned to maintenance with safer alternatives
- For most cosmetic skin specialists, **safer alternatives are the right starting point**, hydroquinone is a referral conversation

**In-clinic adjuncts:**

- **Chemical peels**, superficial peels (mandelic, lactic, low-percentage glycolic) can support topical regimens. Caution in Fitzpatrick IV–VI.
- **Microneedling**, supports topical penetration; cautious settings in deeper skin tones
- **Light-based treatments**, IPL works for lentigines, often inappropriate for melasma (can worsen)
- **Q-switched / picosecond lasers**, specialist territory; refer for advanced melasma cases

## 4. Photoprotection as treatment

In pigmentation, photoprotection isn't aftercare. It's the **primary treatment**.

**Best practice for pigmentation clients:**

- SPF 50 broad-spectrum, daily, reapplied every 2 hours when exposed
- **Mineral or hybrid mineral filters** (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) for melasma, chemical filters protect against UV but not visible light, and visible light drives melasma
- Wide-brimmed hat and sunglasses as physical barriers
- Avoidance of peak UV (10am–4pm) and direct sun
- **Heat avoidance**, saunas, hot showers, prolonged hot environments

Document the photoprotection conversation in every pigmentation consultation. Clients who understand it as treatment (not "skincare advice") follow it.

## 5. Skin-of-colour considerations

Fitzpatrick III–VI clients require specific care:

- **PIH risk is dramatically higher**, every aggressive treatment must be weighed against PIH potential
- **Hydroquinone caution**, ochronosis risk is real
- **Energy-based treatments**, settings must be adjusted; many lasers are unsafe in Fitzpatrick V–VI without specialist training
- **Microneedling and peels**, start at the gentlest end and titrate up
- **Patch testing**, non-negotiable for any new active or in-clinic treatment

**Important:** "Treating pigmentation in dark skin" is a specialist skill set. If you're not confident, refer. A botched melasma plan in Fitzpatrick V can result in PIH that takes 2+ years to resolve.

## 6. Risk of rebound and ethical practice

Many pigmentation conditions rebound when treatment stops. Particularly:

- **Melasma**, relapse is the norm, not the exception. Maintenance plans are essential.
- **Hydroquinone-treated pigmentation**, rebound common after cessation
- **Aggressive bleaching**, can drive worse pigmentation than the original condition

**Ethical practice principles:**

- **Set realistic expectations**, pigmentation rarely fully clears; meaningful improvement is the goal
- **Avoid promising "cure"**, melasma is chronic
- **Maintenance is part of the plan**, not an upsell
- **Avoid over-treating** in Fitzpatrick III–VI

The Skin Specialist's job in pigmentation is **realistic, evidence-based, skin-tone-aware care**, not the most-aggressive bleaching protocol the client read about online.
