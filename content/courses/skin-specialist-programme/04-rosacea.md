Rosacea is the condition that fails on protocols more often than any other. Mostly because the protocol treats the most-visible feature (redness) and ignores the underlying mechanism (vascular, neurological, immunological, barrier-driven).

This module assumes the consultation framework is in place. We're now using it to work through rosacea properly.

## What this module covers

## 1. The four subtypes

Rosacea is a constellation, not a single condition. NICE recognises four subtypes:

**Erythematotelangiectatic (ETR)**, persistent central facial redness, easy flushing, visible telangiectasia. Most common subtype. Most often misdiagnosed as "sensitive skin".

**Papulopustular (PPR)**, redness plus persistent inflammatory papules and pustules. Often misdiagnosed as adult acne. The inflammatory subtype.

**Phymatous**, skin thickening and tissue overgrowth, most commonly nasal (rhinophyma). Slow progression over years. Surgical / laser management often required for established cases.

**Ocular**, burning, itching, dry eyes, foreign-body sensation, recurrent styes / blepharitis. **Most under-recognised subtype** in private cosmetic settings. Refer to ophthalmology.

**Important:** Subtypes are not mutually exclusive. Many clients have features of two or three at once. The treatment pathway changes based on which is dominant, and which subtype is present is a key part of the classification statement.

## 2. The mechanisms, vascular, neurological, immunological

Rosacea is **multi-mechanistic**, which is why single-active treatment so often disappoints.

**Vascular**, facial vessels become dysregulated. Repeated flushing leads to fixed dilation (telangiectasia). Vessels become hypersensitive to triggers. Once telangiectasia is established, topicals rarely fully resolve it, vascular laser (KTP, PDL) is the primary escalation.

**Neurological**, sensory nerves become hyperreactive. Substance P and CGRP signalling drives flushing and tingling. Stress, heat, alcohol activate this neurological axis directly.

**Immunological**, innate immune over-activation. Specifically, abnormal cathelicidin (LL-37) processing at the skin surface drives the inflammatory papules and pustules of papulopustular rosacea.

**Barrier dysfunction**, the stratum corneum in rosacea skin is functionally impaired. TEWL is increased, ceramide content is reduced, and *every* active feels reactive because the barrier isn't holding.

**Microbiome**, *Demodex folliculorum* density is often elevated in papulopustular rosacea, contributing to the inflammatory cascade.

**Clinical principle:** A plan that addresses one mechanism partially helps. A plan that addresses all five compounds. **Barrier first, anti-inflammatory second, vascular and microbial third.**

## 3. Barrier-first vs anti-inflammatory-first, why ordering matters

The most common rosacea plan failure is **starting with the active and skipping the barrier**.

A typical failed plan:

1. Practitioner identifies papulopustular rosacea
2. Prescribes / recommends a sensible barrier-supportive routine + an anti-inflammatory active (e.g. azelaic acid)
3. Client is worse at three weeks, flushed, burning, irritated
4. Plan abandoned, client convinced "nothing works for them"

The plan wasn't wrong. **The order was wrong.** The client's barrier was already compromised. Starting the active before the barrier had recovered crashed the system.

**Barrier-first protocol:**

- **Weeks 1–4**, barrier repair only. Gentle cleanser. Ceramide / fatty-acid moisturiser twice daily. SPF every day. No actives.
- **Weeks 4–8**, introduce one anti-inflammatory active (most commonly azelaic acid 10–15% at first, ramping to 20% if tolerated). Maintain barrier support.
- **Weeks 8+**, adjust based on response. Add second active only if first is well tolerated. Consider topical metronidazole or ivermectin for inflammatory subtypes.
- **Long-term**, vascular laser referral for fixed telangiectasia. Lifelong barrier maintenance.

This protocol has a fundamentally different success rate than the active-first version. Same actives, same skin, different order, different outcome.

## 4. The topical pathway

For papulopustular rosacea, the evidence-based topical ladder:

- **Azelaic acid 15–20%**, first-line, addresses inflammation + mild antimicrobial
- **Topical metronidazole**, reduces papules and pustules over 8–12 weeks
- **Topical ivermectin**, addresses *Demodex* density, reduces inflammatory lesions
- **Brimonidine** (vasoconstrictor), for episodic erythema; use cautiously, can rebound
- **Oral doxycycline** (sub-antimicrobial dose), referral / GP-prescribed for moderate-severe inflammatory subtypes

For erythematotelangiectatic rosacea:

- Barrier support + photoprotection are foundational
- Topical brimonidine for episodic erythema
- **Vascular laser** (KTP / PDL) for fixed telangiectasia, refer to laser specialist

For phymatous rosacea, refer dermatology / surgical specialist.

For ocular rosacea, refer ophthalmology.

## 5. Trigger-mapping protocol

The trigger conversation is rarely framed in a way clients act on. The Skin Specialist trigger-mapping protocol:

**Step 1: Trigger inventory** at consultation. Ask specifically about:

- Sun exposure
- Heat (hot drinks, hot showers, hot weather)
- Cold (cold air, wind)
- Alcohol (especially red wine, spirits)
- Spicy food
- Stress / emotional triggers
- Skincare actives
- Exercise

**Step 2: Diary for 2 weeks.** Client records flares + recent triggers. Print template in the supporting materials.

**Step 3: Pattern review.** Together at follow-up. Identify the 2-3 most-reactive triggers for *this client*.

**Step 4: Practical management plan.** Not "avoid all triggers", that's unrealistic. Instead: "manage the top 2-3 triggers with specific tactics" (cool drink before hot food, stress-management technique before known stressors, mineral-based SPF every day).

**Why this works:** Specific, evidence-based, non-prescriptive. Clients who understand their own pattern follow the plan. Clients told to "avoid triggers" generally don't.

## 6. Ocular rosacea, the missed subtype

Up to 50% of rosacea cases have ocular involvement. Most are missed in cosmetic consultations because the practitioner focuses on the facial skin and forgets to ask.

**Symptoms to ask about:**

- Burning, itching, dry eyes
- Foreign-body sensation ("something in my eye")
- Recurrent styes or blepharitis
- Light sensitivity
- Blurred vision (less common)

**Refer to ophthalmology** for:

- Persistent ocular symptoms
- Any visual changes
- Suspected blepharitis with cosmetic implications

Document the ocular history in every rosacea consultation. If absent, document its absence ("no ocular symptoms reported"). This is part of the defensible-practice file.
