Scarring is the condition where managing expectations is more important than the treatment itself. Almost no scar fully resolves. Realistic, structured, multi-modal management is what differentiates a Skin Specialist from a practitioner who over-promises.

## What this module covers

- Scar classification (ice-pick, boxcar, rolling, hypertrophic, keloid)
- Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) vs post-inflammatory erythema (PIE)
- Microneedling, peels, light-based options
- When to refer (deep dermal scarring, keloid risk)
- Photographic documentation of progress
- Setting realistic timelines (12 months+)

## 1. Scar classification

**Atrophic scars**, depressed, dermal-loss scars. Most common in post-acne scarring.

- **Ice-pick scars**, narrow, deep, sharp-edged. Hardest to treat.
- **Boxcar scars**, wider, U-shaped, defined edges. Moderate treatment response.
- **Rolling scars**, broad, undulating depressions, fibrous tethering underneath. Best response to combination therapy.

**Hypertrophic scars**, raised, within original wound boundary. More common after deep inflammatory acne, surgical incisions, deep procedures.

**Keloid scars**, raised, extending beyond original wound boundary. Genetic predisposition, more common in Fitzpatrick V-VI. **Caution in any in-clinic procedure**, risk of triggering keloid response.

**Mixed scarring**, most clients have multiple types simultaneously. Document each with photographs and a treatment plan addressing each.

## 2. Post-inflammatory pigmentation (PIH) and erythema (PIE)

Often confused but **completely different**.

**PIH** (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation):

- Brown to dark-brown discolouration where lesions resolved
- Caused by melanocyte activity in response to inflammation
- More common in Fitzpatrick III-VI
- **Treatment:** photoprotection + tyrosinase inhibitors + time (6-18 months for natural resolution)

**PIE** (post-inflammatory erythema):

- Pink to red discolouration where lesions resolved
- Caused by superficial vascular dilation, not pigment
- More common in Fitzpatrick I-III
- **Treatment:** time + barrier support + occasionally vascular laser. Tyrosinase inhibitors don't help PIE.

**Common error:** Treating PIE as PIH with tyrosinase inhibitors. Doesn't work because there's no pigment to inhibit.

**Diagnostic tip:** PIH is brown / dark. PIE is pink / red. PIH does not blanch under glass slide pressure; PIE does (because PIE is vascular).

## 3. The treatment toolkit

**Topical:**

- **Retinoids**, slow, modest effect on atrophic scars over 6-12+ months
- **Tyrosinase inhibitors** (azelaic, kojic, tranexamic, niacinamide), for PIH
- **Silicone gel sheets**, first-line for hypertrophic and keloid scars
- **Vitamin C**, supportive antioxidant + collagen support

**In-clinic, atrophic scars:**

- **Microneedling** (CIT), first-line for rolling and shallow boxcar scars. 3-6 sessions. Modest improvement (20-40%) typical.
- **Chemical peels** (medium-depth TCA, CROSS technique for ice-pick), specialist procedural
- **Subcision**, for tethered rolling scars; specialist procedural
- **Fractional laser** (non-ablative or ablative), strong evidence for atrophic scars; specialist territory
- **Dermal fillers**, for individual deep boxcar / rolling scars (specialist refer-out for skin specialists)

**In-clinic, hypertrophic / keloid scars:**

- **Silicone gel sheets**, first-line, daily for months
- **Intralesional steroid injection**, refer to dermatology
- **Cryotherapy**, refer to dermatology
- **Laser** (PDL, fractional), specialist territory

**Important:** A skin-specialist's role in scarring is often to **establish the multi-modal plan, photograph progress, and refer for the procedural elements** they're not trained for. Honest scope of practice matters.

## 4. When to refer

- **Established hypertrophic / keloid scarring**, dermatology
- **Active scarring during inflammatory flare**, dermatology (treat the underlying condition first)
- **Deep dermal atrophic scarring**, fractional laser specialist
- **Surgical-scar revision questions**, plastic surgery / scar specialist
- **Significant psychological impact** of scarring, mental health support

## 5. Photographic documentation of progress

Scarring is the condition where photographs matter most:

- Same lighting, same angles, same expression, every visit
- Cross-polarised lighting if available (highlights texture)
- Side-lighting (45°) for atrophic scar shadow detail
- Document at baseline, every 4 weeks, every 12 weeks, annually
- Share photographic comparisons with the client at follow-ups, visible progress sustains compliance through long timelines

## 6. Setting realistic timelines

**The conversation that anchors every scarring plan:**

- **Atrophic scars**, 6-month minimum to evaluate any treatment response. 12 months for meaningful improvement. Multi-modal multi-year approach for significant cases.
- **Hypertrophic scars**, 6-12 months of silicone + steroid response evaluation
- **Keloid scars**, long-term management, recurrence common, never fully cured
- **PIH**, 6-18 months for natural resolution; topicals accelerate but don't shortcut
- **PIE**, 6-12 months typically; can be shorter with vascular laser

**Realistic improvement language:**

- "20-40% improvement is a good outcome for atrophic acne scarring"
- "Most scars improve, some persist; full resolution is rare"
- "Maintenance is part of the plan, not an upsell"

**Common practitioner error:** Promising "smooth skin" for an established scarring case. Sets up the client for disappointment and damages your reputation when reality lands.
