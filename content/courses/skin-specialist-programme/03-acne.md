## 1. The four mechanisms revisited

Every active acne lesion is the visible end of four interlocking processes:

1. **Excess sebum production**, androgens stimulate the sebaceous gland
2. **Follicular hyperkeratinisation**, keratinocytes plug the pore (microcomedone)
3. **Cutibacterium acnes dysbiosis**, the anaerobic environment lets *C. acnes* over-proliferate
4. **Inflammation**, the immune response producing visible papules, pustules, nodules

**Clinical principle:** A plan that treats one or two of these four mechanisms partially helps. A plan that treats all four compounds. Most "stalled" acne plans are stalled because they're addressing two mechanisms when they need to be addressing four.

## 2. Hormonal-pattern acne in adult women

Adult-onset acne in women follows a recognisable pattern. Once you can spot it, you can't un-spot it:

- **Distribution**, jawline, chin, neck, sometimes upper neck
- **Cyclical pattern**, premenstrual flare in 60-70% of cases
- **Lesion type**, predominantly inflammatory papules and small nodules; comedones may be sparse
- **Onset**, late twenties / early thirties / perimenopause
- **Trigger profile**, stress-reactive, often resistant to over-the-counter regimens

**Clinical relevance:** Topical-only management of hormonal-pattern acne in adult women has a low success ceiling. The plan needs to either:

- Include a **referral conversation** about hormonal pathway management (combined oral contraceptive, spironolactone, GP / endocrinologist input)
- **Set realistic expectations** that topical-only addresses 30-50% of the picture
- Consider **PCOS screening** if the pattern is severe, persistent, or accompanied by other PCOS markers (hirsutism, irregular cycles, weight changes)

**Best practice:** Document the hormonal-pattern recognition explicitly in the classification statement. Document the referral conversation if you have it. If you don't have it, document why not.

## 3. Severity grading + escalation triggers (NICE-aligned)

NICE acne guidance grades severity as:

- **Mild**, predominantly comedones, few inflammatory lesions
- **Moderate**, multiple papules and pustules, with comedones
- **Severe**, nodules, widespread inflammation, scarring, significant psychosocial impact

**Escalation triggers** (refer to GP / dermatology):

- Severe acne by NICE definition
- Active scarring at any severity (atrophic or hypertrophic)
- Significant psychosocial impact (mood, social withdrawal, body dysmorphic features)
- Treatment failure after 12 weeks of an evidence-based plan
- Suspected hormonal pathway requiring medication
- Requesting / requiring isotretinoin

**Important:** Scarring risk alone justifies escalation, even when acne appears "mild" by lesion count. NICE explicitly weights this. Document the escalation trigger and the referral.

## 4. AHAs, BHAs, retinoids, sequencing

The active sequence matters as much as the actives themselves.

**AHAs (alpha-hydroxy acids, glycolic, lactic, mandelic)**

- Water-soluble, work at the skin surface
- Improve texture, support cell turnover, help post-acne pigmentation
- Best for surface-level changes
- **Risk:** over-exfoliation, barrier compromise

**BHAs (beta-hydroxy acids, salicylic acid)**

- Oil-soluble, work inside the pore
- Direct action on follicular blockage and *C. acnes* environment
- Best for comedonal and early-inflammatory acne
- **Risk:** dryness if over-used

**Retinoids (adapalene, tretinoin, retinol)**

- Affect cell turnover and the early stages of comedogenesis
- The single most-evidence-based topical for acne
- Effective across all severity levels (with appropriate strength)
- **Risk:** retinoid dermatitis if introduced too aggressively

**Sequencing rules:**

- Introduce one active at a time, every 2–4 weeks
- Always lead with **barrier support** before introducing actives
- BHA + retinoid is a common evidence-based combination; avoid AHA + retinoid concurrently
- SPF is non-negotiable, every plan, every day

**Common mistake:** Stacking three actives in week one to "accelerate results". This crashes the barrier, the immune response goes into overdrive, and the client looks worse before they look better, and many quit.

## 5. Post-acne pigmentation (PIH) and scarring risk

**Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)**, flat brown / red marks where lesions have resolved. Common in Fitzpatrick III–VI, possible at any skin tone.

- Tincture of time (6–18 months for natural resolution)
- Photoprotection is foundational (sun darkens PIH)
- Tyrosinase inhibitors (azelaic acid, kojic acid, niacinamide, tranexamic acid topical)
- Avoid aggressive treatments early, PIH can be worsened by over-stimulation

**Atrophic scarring**, ice-pick, boxcar, rolling. Permanent dermal damage.

- Risk highest in moderate-to-severe inflammatory and nodular acne
- Picking, squeezing, and aggressive in-clinic procedures during active flare increase risk
- Once formed, scarring is treatable but not erasable, microneedling, peels, dermal fillers, laser. Multi-modality.

**Hypertrophic / keloid scarring**, raised scars, more common in deeper skin tones.

- Refer to dermatology for established hypertrophic acne scarring
- Caution with in-clinic procedures (microneedling, peels), risk of triggering keloid response

**Best practice:** Photograph PIH and scarring at every consultation. Document the scarring risk in the classification statement. The risk drives the plan.

## 6. When to refer

Referral is professional responsibility, not failure. NICE-aligned escalation pathways:

- **GP**, for hormonal pathway evaluation, OCP / spironolactone consideration, mental health support
- **Dermatology**, for severe acne, treatment-resistant moderate acne, scarring, isotretinoin candidacy
- **Endocrinology**, for suspected PCOS, hirsutism, menstrual irregularities + acne
- **Mental health**, for body dysmorphic features, significant mood impact, social withdrawal

Document every referral conversation. The conversation is part of the plan.
