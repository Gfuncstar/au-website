## Why one protocol does not fit five conditions

The biggest mistake in hyperpigmentation is treating it as a single condition. It isn't. What practitioners call "pigmentation" is at least five distinct presentations, each with a different driver, a different depth, and a different protocol.

Get the presentation right at the consultation, and the rest of the plan follows. Get it wrong, and you will spend twelve weeks treating the wrong thing.

This module gives you a face-reading framework that works in clinic, on real clients, in the first thirty seconds.

## The five presentations

### 1. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)

The pigment left behind after inflammation. Acne is the most common cause, but anything that triggers an inflammatory cascade can leave PIH: a rash, a peel that went too deep, mechanical trauma, an aggressive retinoid, an ingrown hair.

**Visual clues**

- Sits in the same locations the inflammation sat (jawline, cheeks, hairline if acne-driven)
- Tends to be darker on higher Fitzpatrick skin types and more persistent
- Often appears as discrete macules tracking individual lesions
- Surface texture usually normal, sometimes mildly atrophic where the inflammation was severe

**Protocol implication**

PIH responds well to **gentle** tyrosinase inhibition and patient barrier work. The trap is over-treating, which restarts the inflammation and deepens the pigment. PIH protocols are slower than the client expects. The consultation has to set that timeline at the start.

### 2. Melasma

A patterned, often symmetrical hyperpigmentation driven by hormones, sun and visible light. Most common in women aged 20–50, more prevalent in higher Fitzpatrick types, and notoriously prone to rebound.

**Visual clues**

- One of three distribution patterns: **centrofacial** (forehead, upper lip, chin, nose), **malar** (cheekbones), or **mandibular** (along the jaw)
- Tends to be symmetrical
- Diffuse rather than discrete (no clean borders)
- Often worsens after sun exposure, pregnancy, or starting / changing hormonal contraception

**Protocol implication**

Melasma is the presentation most likely to **rebound** after aggressive in-clinic work. It is treatable, but it is treated like a chronic condition: long-game, year-round, sun protection at the centre. IPL and aggressive lasers are usually the wrong call. Module 6 covers the modality map.

### 3. Solar lentigines and photo-induced epidermal pigment

The "age spots" and diffuse mottled pigment from cumulative UV exposure. Sits in the **epidermis**, which is why it responds well to surface-level treatment.

**Visual clues**

- Defined borders, often irregular but not raised
- Most common on cheeks, temples, dorsal hands, chest
- Pattern follows lifelong sun exposure, not a recent flare
- Often appears alongside textural sun damage (lentigines plus crepiness)

**Protocol implication**

Epidermal pigment responds well to **peels, light-based treatment, and tyrosinase inhibition**. Of the five presentations, this is the one most likely to clear with one or two well-sequenced procedures. The consultation conversation is about realistic post-procedure compliance and SPF.

### 4. Dermal and mixed pigment

Pigment that has dropped beneath the epidermis into the dermis, or sits across both layers. This is the presentation most often misdiagnosed and most often missed at consultation.

**Visual clues**

- Greyish or bluish tone rather than brown (the optical effect of melanin sitting deeper)
- Less response to topicals than expected
- Drug-induced pigmentation (minocycline, antimalarials, NSAIDs, amiodarone) tends to live here
- Some genetic and post-inflammatory pigmentations end up dermal after years untreated

**Protocol implication**

Dermal pigment will not respond to a tyrosinase inhibitor alone. It needs a **two-phase plan**: epidermal preparation, then a depth-appropriate intervention. The Wood's lamp test in Module 2 is what surfaces this.

### 5. Post-inflammatory erythema, and conditions that get treated as pigment

The presentation most often **mis-treated**. Post-inflammatory erythema (PIE) is the red, vascular shadow left behind after inflammation, particularly in lower Fitzpatrick types. It looks like pigment but it isn't melanin. Treating it as melanin makes it worse.

There are also conditions that get walked into a pigmentation consultation but are something else entirely: rosacea, melanocytic naevi, lichen planus pigmentosus, Riehl's melanosis, ochronosis from over-treated melasma. If a presentation does not fit one of the four melanin patterns above, the right answer is often a referral, not a topical.

**Protocol implication**

If it is PIE, the plan is **vascular and barrier**, not lightening. If it is something else, the plan is **diagnose first, treat second** — and sometimes diagnose-and-refer.

## How to use this in the consultation

In the first thirty seconds you are looking for:

1. **Distribution.** Is it patterned (melasma) or scattered (PIH, lentigines)?
2. **Borders.** Discrete (lentigines, PIH) or diffuse (melasma, mixed)?
3. **Tone.** Brown (epidermal melanin) or grey-blue (dermal melanin)? Red, not brown (PIE)?
4. **History.** Hormonal life stage, recent inflammation, drug history, sun history.
5. **What is the surrounding skin doing?** A face that is also rosacea-prone changes the plan.

You don't have to lock the diagnosis in thirty seconds. You have to know which of the five tracks you are running so the rest of the consultation is the right shape.
