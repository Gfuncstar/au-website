## The principle: modality follows presentation, not the other way around

The most common modality mistake in pigmentation is starting with the device the clinic owns and looking for clients to put through it. That works for some businesses. It does not work for hyperpigmentation, where the wrong device on the wrong presentation makes things worse.

This module gives you the modality map, structured by presentation.

## The decision tree

Before any procedural decision, run the following checks:

1. **Which of the five presentations is this?** (Module 1.)
2. **What depth is the pigment?** (Module 2.)
3. **What's the systemic driver?** (Module 3, if relevant.)
4. **Is the client tolerating their topical layer?** (Module 5.)
5. **What season is it?** (Module 4.)
6. **What's the client's UV-protection compliance like?**

If you cannot answer those six confidently, the answer to "should we do a procedure?" is **not yet**.

## Chemical peels

Light to medium-depth peels can be highly effective for **epidermal pigmentation**.

### Light peels (mandelic, lactic, low-percentage glycolic)

- **Best for:** PIH on resilient skin. Light epidermal pigment. Maintenance protocol for melasma in winter.
- **Avoid for:** Active melasma flare. Compromised barrier. Higher Fitzpatrick types until tolerance to lower percentages is established.
- **Cadence:** Series of 4–6 sessions, two to three weeks apart.

### Medium peels (TCA at low percentages, Jessner's, modified Jessner)

- **Best for:** Resistant epidermal pigment. Solar lentigines. Some PIH cases.
- **Avoid for:** Melasma at meaningful percentages. Higher Fitzpatrick types without prior priming. Active inflammation.
- **Cadence:** Single treatments with significant recovery; not a series.

### Deep peels (high-percentage TCA, phenol)

- **Outside the scope** of most non-medical aesthetics clinics. Refer.

### Cautions specific to melasma

Peels can flare melasma. The flare-versus-clear ratio depends on:

- **Fitzpatrick.** Higher Fitzpatricks have higher rebound risk. Mandelic and PHA are safer first choices.
- **Driver.** Active hormonal driver = higher risk.
- **Season.** Summer = higher risk.
- **Barrier.** Compromised barrier = higher risk.

A useful rule: in melasma, **the lightest peel that produces a result is the right peel**.

## Microneedling

A controversial modality in pigmentation. The evidence-base is mixed and clinical outcomes vary widely.

### When it helps

- Post-acne pigmentation with associated atrophic scarring (the texture work helps).
- Drug delivery (microneedling plus topical tranexamic acid has emerging evidence for melasma).
- Resilient PIH.

### When it triggers PIH

- Higher Fitzpatricks, deeper depths, more aggressive cadence.
- Compromised barrier.
- Active inflammation.
- Done close to peak-UV season without strict aftercare.

### Sensible parameters

- **Depth: 0.5–1.5mm** for pigmentation work. Deeper depths increase trauma without proportional benefit.
- **Cadence: 4–6 weeks** between sessions.
- **Series: 3–4 sessions** maximum, then reassess.
- **Aftercare:** strict SPF, no actives for 3–5 days, no heat for 48 hours.

If you are unsure whether microneedling fits a client, **defer**. There are slower-but-safer paths.

## Laser and IPL

The most outcome-variable category, and the easiest to get wrong.

### IPL (intense pulsed light)

Broadband light, multiple wavelengths, targets melanin and haemoglobin chromophores.

- **Best for:** Solar lentigines. Photoaging. Some defined epidermal pigment in lower Fitzpatrick types.
- **Use cautiously for:** PIH on lower Fitzpatricks, when the texture also needs work.
- **Avoid for:** **Melasma**, almost without exception. **Higher Fitzpatrick types** generally (heat-based light on higher melanin density carries high PIH risk).

The most common pigmentation mistake practitioners make is putting an IPL through a melasma client because "it worked on the lentigines patient last week". The melasma melanocyte is hyperactive. Heat sensitises it further. The pigment comes back darker.

### Q-switched lasers (532nm, 1064nm)

Selective targeting of melanin in pigmented lesions. Less heat than IPL.

- **Best for:** Discrete lentigines. Some PIH cases by experienced operators.
- **Cautiously for:** Melasma — low-fluence 1064nm Q-switched (the "laser toning" protocol) has some evidence in melasma but rebound risk remains.
- **Avoid for:** Melasma without significant operator experience. Compromised barrier. Untreated hormonal driver.

### Picosecond lasers

The most precise category, with shortest pulse durations and least thermal spread.

- **Best for:** Resistant pigment, including some melasma in skilled hands.
- **Caveats:** Operator-dependent. Higher cost. Same Fitzpatrick and seasonal cautions apply.

### Fractional non-ablative lasers

- **Best for:** Photoaging, some PIH cases with associated texture concerns.
- **Variable for:** Melasma. Some operators report success, others see flares. Pre-treat with a calm topical protocol.

### Vascular lasers (PDL, KTP)

- **Best for:** PIE (the "not actually pigment" red presentation from Module 1). Erythema, telangiectasia.
- **Not for:** Melanin pigment.

## The combination question

Practitioners often ask: can I peel and microneedle on the same client? Can I laser and use a topical retinoid?

The answer is yes, with sequencing and spacing. A reasonable order of operations for a complex pigmentation case:

1. **Eight weeks of barrier and topical work first.** Build tolerance. Confirm compliance. See how much moves on topicals alone.
2. **First in-clinic procedure** chosen based on the dominant presentation. Lightest effective option.
3. **Six to eight weeks recovery** before the next procedure.
4. **Reassess at twelve weeks** with photo evidence. Adjust.

What does **not** work: peel one week, microneedling the next, laser the week after. The recovery and protective windows overlap and the cumulative inflammation drives more pigment than any single procedure can clear.

## Combinations to refuse

Some combinations should be refused at consultation:

- **Active melasma + IPL** (almost always wrong)
- **Compromised barrier + any heat-based device** (rebuild first)
- **Active hormonal driver + aggressive procedural plan + summer** (defer to autumn)
- **Higher Fitzpatrick + aggressive lasers without operator experience** (refer)
- **Pregnancy + any pigment-active procedural** (defer to post-partum and post-breastfeeding)
- **Suspected ochronosis from chronic hydroquinone use + more lightening procedures** (full stop and reassess)

Refusing the wrong procedure is one of the highest-trust moves you can make in clinic. The client who hears "this isn't the right modality for what you have" is the client who tells ten friends about your judgement.
