## Why depth is the question that changes the plan

Two clients come into clinic with brown pigment on their cheeks. Same Fitzpatrick. Same age. Same product history. One of them clears on a topical alone in twelve weeks. The other doesn't move on the same protocol over six months and quietly disappears.

The difference is depth. The first client's pigment was epidermal. The second client's pigment was sitting deeper, in the upper dermis, where a topical cannot reach the melanocyte that is making it.

Depth is the single most useful thing to read off a face before you write a plan. This module gives you the framework.

## The two melanins, briefly

Melanin is not one substance. It is a family.

- **Eumelanin.** Brown to black. Photoprotective. The dominant melanin in higher Fitzpatrick skin types and in classic hyperpigmentation patterns.
- **Pheomelanin.** Yellow to red. Less photoprotective. More common in lower Fitzpatrick types, and the reason fair-skinned clients with red and freckled tones tend to scar erythematous (PIE) rather than pigment (PIH).

For most hyperpigmentation work, you are managing eumelanin. The tone (brown vs grey-blue) tells you depth more reliably than it tells you which melanin.

## The three depths that matter in clinic

### Epidermal pigment

Lives in the **upper layers of the skin**: stratum basale up through the granular layer.

- **Visual cue.** Sharp brown tone. Can sit as discrete macules (lentigines) or diffuse patches (some melasma).
- **Wood's lamp.** The pigment **enhances** under Wood's lamp. The borders look more defined.
- **Topical response.** Good. Tyrosinase inhibitors, exfoliants, retinoids and antioxidants reach the melanocyte.
- **Procedural response.** Excellent. Light, peels, microneedling can all clear epidermal pigment if matched to the presentation.

### Dermal pigment

Lives **beneath the dermo-epidermal junction**, in the upper dermis. Pigment-laden melanophages have either migrated down or been deposited there directly.

- **Visual cue.** Grey-blue or muddy tone rather than clean brown. The pigment looks "deeper" because it is.
- **Wood's lamp.** The pigment **does not enhance** under Wood's lamp. Borders blur.
- **Topical response.** Limited. The melanocyte producing it is below the reach of most topical actives.
- **Procedural response.** Variable, and risk-laden. Aggressive heat-based devices can damage the dermal architecture and trigger PIH on top of the existing dermal pigment.

### Mixed depth

Both. Most chronic melasma. Most older PIH that has been left untreated for years.

- **Visual cue.** Brown-on-grey, or patches that show different tone density across the same lesion.
- **Wood's lamp.** Mixed enhancement. Some areas brighten, others stay dull.
- **Plan implication.** Two-phase. Treat the epidermal layer first. Reassess at week eight to twelve. Add a depth-appropriate procedural step only when the surface is calm.

## The Wood's lamp, in plain language

A Wood's lamp emits longwave UV at around 365nm. In a darkened room, held about 10cm from the skin:

- **Epidermal pigment becomes more pronounced** — it absorbs the UV and the contrast against surrounding skin goes up.
- **Dermal pigment becomes less pronounced** — the melanin is too deep for the UV to reach efficiently, so the contrast washes out.
- **Mixed pigment shows mixed enhancement** — some areas brighten, others soften.

This is not a perfect test. It works best on lower Fitzpatrick skin types. Above Fitzpatrick V the contrast becomes harder to read because background eumelanin is high. But on most clients it gives you, in twenty seconds, the answer to the most important question of the consultation: **how deep is this?**

## Explaining depth to the client

Most clients have never been told their pigment lives somewhere. They've been sold "brightening serums" and treatments that imply the melanin is on the surface and can be wiped off.

A useful script:

> The pigment you can see is being made by a cell beneath the surface. In your case it is sitting [closer to / deeper than] the layer most products can reach. That changes which products are going to work, and how long the timeline is. Here is what that means for your plan.

The conversation reframes the timeline before the client expects it to. It also reframes a slow-moving result as **expected**, not a failure of the protocol.

## Where this leads

Module 3 takes you out of the skin layer and into the systemic driver. For most melasma, and for a non-trivial subset of stubborn PIH, the pigment is being **made faster** than any topical can clear it because the melanocyte is being switched on hormonally. If you don't surface that at the consultation, you will be treating the face while the body keeps making more.

When you are ready, move on.
