## Why rebound is the silent killer of pigmentation outcomes

The most common reason a previously-cleared client returns darker than they started is not that the original procedure was wrong. It is that the **post-procedure window** was not protected.

You can do a perfect peel. The skin can heal cleanly. And then, in the next ten to fourteen days, one careless restart of an active, one beach holiday, one hot shower, one ill-timed flare in a hormonal driver, and the same melanocyte that was settling down lights up again, harder.

Rebound is preventable. This module is the protocol.

## The five rebound triggers

In order of how often they show up:

### 1. Sun and visible light

The most common rebound trigger. Skin coming out of a procedure has reduced melanin in the treated area, which means less photoprotection from its own pigment. UV and visible light exposure on this skin drives compensatory melanin production faster than baseline.

A client who treats hyperpigmentation in May and travels in June without a strict UV plan is the classic rebound case.

### 2. Restarting actives too soon

Retinoids, AHAs, and high-percentage tyrosinase inhibitors restarted before the skin has fully recovered will trigger an inflammatory cascade and PIH. The client typically restarts because they "want to keep momentum". The cost is the result.

### 3. Heat exposure

Hot showers, saunas, hot yoga, intense exercise, hot drinks. Heat is a vasodilator and a melanocyte sensitiser, particularly in melasma. A two-week heat-light protocol after any procedural work is critical.

### 4. Hormonal flare

A pre-existing hormonal driver that wasn't fully accounted for at consultation can flare in the post-procedure window. Most often: late menstrual cycle, recent contraceptive change, perimenopausal swing, undiagnosed thyroid event.

This is the trigger most often missed because it isn't visible. A client whose pigment rebounded "for no reason" usually has one of these in their history if you go back and ask.

### 5. Mechanical trauma

Picking, exfoliating with sponges or cloths, aggressive cleansing, friction from clothing, sleeping on a coarse pillowcase. Anything that re-traumatises the skin in the recovery window can trigger PIH on top of the procedural site.

## The two-week protective protocol

Apply this to **any** procedural work that has any inflammatory component (peels, microneedling, laser, IPL).

### Days 1–3 (acute recovery)

- **Cleansing.** Lukewarm water, gentle non-foaming cleanser, fingertips only, pat dry.
- **Topicals.** Barrier-supportive moisturiser. Nothing else. No actives.
- **Sun and light.** Strict avoidance. Tinted SPF in the clinic before the client leaves, every two hours indoors and out for 72 hours.
- **Heat.** No hot showers, no saunas, no hot yoga, no intense exercise. Cool to lukewarm only.
- **Sleep.** Clean pillowcase, sleep on back if possible, no face pressed into bedding.
- **No makeup** unless mineral, applied with clean fingers, on day three at the earliest.

### Days 4–7

- Continue gentle cleansing and barrier moisturiser.
- Continue strict tinted SPF.
- Heat avoidance continues.
- Light exercise OK if face stays cool. No sweating onto the treated area.
- **Antioxidant** (vitamin C derivative or niacinamide) can be reintroduced from day five if barrier is intact.

### Days 8–14

- Reassess at day eight in clinic if possible. Photo documentation.
- **Tyrosinase inhibitor** can be reintroduced gently from day eight if barrier is intact and no active inflammation.
- **Exfoliants and retinoids: not yet.** Earliest reintroduction is day fourteen, and only if barrier is robust.
- Heat caution continues, particularly for melasma clients.

### Day 15 onwards

- Slow re-introduction of the full topical sequence, adding back one active per week.
- Continue tinted SPF every day, year-round.
- Reassess at four weeks post-procedure. Plan next steps.

## The recovery script for each rebound trigger

If a client comes in three weeks after a procedure with the pigment darker than baseline, your first move is **not** another procedure. It is to find the trigger.

| Trigger | What to ask | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| Sun | Have you been in stronger UV than usual? Travel? Lots of outdoor time? Tinted SPF compliance? | Pause all actives for 48 hours. Strict tinted SPF. Cool compresses if irritated. Reassess in two weeks. |
| Active restart | Did you restart any active products (retinoid, acid, brightener) in the last two weeks? | Stop them. Barrier-only protocol for two weeks. Restart at one product per week. |
| Heat | Any hot showers, saunas, hot yoga, intense exercise, recent illness with fever? | Heat avoidance for two weeks. Cool to lukewarm only. Reassess. |
| Hormonal | Where is the client in their cycle? Any contraceptive change? Pregnancy possibility? Recent stress / sleep disruption? | Note the trigger, no procedural escalation, reassess at the next stable hormonal point (usually post-period or week six of next cycle phase). |
| Mechanical | Any new exfoliant, cleansing tool, friction source (mask, scarf, pillowcase)? | Remove the offender. Barrier protocol for two weeks. |

In all five cases the pattern is the same: **identify, withdraw, calm, then reassess**. Not escalate.

## The "we have made it worse" conversation

This is the conversation no practitioner wants to have, and the one that defines whether you keep the client.

A useful structure:

> What you're seeing is rebound, not a failure of the procedure. Here's what triggered it, here's what we're going to do for the next two weeks to settle it, and here's what we'll know at our next reassessment. The plan is unchanged in shape, but the timeline has moved by about four weeks. I want to be straightforward with you that this can happen, particularly with [their specific risk factor], and the next decision is about pulling the plan back to the gentlest possible setting until the skin has reset.

What that does:

- Names the rebound clearly (the client already knows something is wrong).
- Identifies the trigger (so it doesn't get blamed on the procedure or the practitioner).
- Gives a plan and a timeline.
- Acknowledges the disappointment without apologising for the underlying clinical decision.

Clients respect that conversation more than they respect a defensive one. The clinics that retain hyperpigmentation clients long-term are the ones that handle this well.
