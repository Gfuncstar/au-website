## Why melasma is the presentation that defeats topical-only protocols

Of the five presentations covered in Module 1, melasma is the one that most consistently fails on a topical-only plan. The reason isn't the topical. It's that melasma is being driven from somewhere a topical cannot reach: the **endocrine system**.

Treat melasma as a skin problem and you will be running uphill. Surface the systemic driver at the consultation, and the plan starts holding.

This module gives you the history-taking framework to do that in fifteen minutes.

## What's actually happening underneath melasma

Melasma is the result of a **hyperactivated melanocyte**, made hyperactive by a combination of three inputs:

1. **Hormonal signalling**, particularly oestrogen and progesterone, which up-regulate melanocyte-stimulating hormone (MSH) receptors and increase tyrosinase activity.
2. **Light exposure**, including UVA, UVB, and **visible light** (especially the high-energy visible blue range, around 400–500nm).
3. **Heat and inflammation**, which sensitise the same melanocyte further.

Hormones set the gain. Light triggers the burst. Heat amplifies it.

When you are running a melasma consultation, you are looking for the hormone events that turned the gain up. A topical alone cannot turn the gain back down. A topical plus a year-round sun and visible-light strategy plus identifying the hormonal trigger gives the plan a chance.

## The hormonal life-events to surface

These are the events that most often correlate with melasma onset or flare. Run them at the consultation.

### Pregnancy

Melasma is so commonly triggered by pregnancy that it has been called "the mask of pregnancy" (chloasma) for decades. The combined surge in oestrogen, progesterone, and MSH during pregnancy can switch melasma on for the first time, often in the second or third trimester.

**Ask:** any pregnancies? When did the pigment start, relative to the pregnancy timeline? Did it fade after birth or persist?

### Combined oral contraceptive (COC) and HRT

Synthetic oestrogen and progestogen do the same thing pregnancy does, more steadily. The COC is a frequent melasma trigger, particularly within the first six to twelve months of starting it or after switching brands.

HRT, particularly the combined formulations and the higher-oestrogen patches, can trigger or flare melasma in the perimenopausal and postmenopausal client.

**Ask:** what hormonal contraception or HRT have you been on, and when did you start each? Did the pigment appear or worsen within twelve months of a change?

You are not asking the client to stop their contraceptive or HRT. You are asking so the **plan accounts for it**. A topical plan with no hormonal context will fail.

### Perimenopause

The unstable-oestrogen window. As ovarian function fluctuates, oestrogen swings up and down rather than dropping smoothly. Those swings can trigger melasma in clients who have never had pigment problems before.

**Ask:** any cycle changes, mood changes, sleep changes, vasomotor symptoms in the last two to five years?

### Thyroid involvement

A meaningful subset of melasma clients have undiagnosed thyroid dysfunction (most often subclinical hypothyroidism, sometimes Hashimoto's). The thyroid axis modulates pigment in ways that are still being characterised in the literature, but the clinical correlation is established enough to ask.

**Ask:** any thyroid history, family history of thyroid disease, fatigue / cold intolerance / unexplained weight changes / hair thinning?

If the answer is yes and the client has not had thyroid function tests in the last twelve months, **refer back to the GP** before you start a melasma protocol. You are not diagnosing thyroid disease in your clinic. You are noting that the systemic driver may be unaddressed and the protocol will under-deliver until it is.

### Stress, sleep and inflammatory load

Cortisol modulates the same axis. Chronically poor sleep, chronic stress, and chronic inflammatory states (rosacea, eczema, autoimmune conditions, long COVID) all keep the melanocyte sensitised.

**Ask:** how is sleep? Has anything been particularly stressful in the last two years? Any chronic inflammatory conditions?

You can't fix a client's stress in clinic. You can name it as a contributor to the timeline so it doesn't get blamed on the protocol.

## The fifteen-minute history, structured

A useful sequence at the consultation:

1. **When did you first notice the pigment?** (Anchor the timeline.)
2. **What was happening in your life around that time?** (Open question, lets the driver surface.)
3. **Pregnancies, contraception, HRT — past and current.** (Specific.)
4. **Cycle and perimenopausal symptoms.** (Specific, age-appropriate.)
5. **Thyroid and other endocrine history.** (Specific.)
6. **Sleep, stress and inflammation.** (Lifestyle.)
7. **Any prior treatment for the pigment, and what happened.** (What's already failed.)

By the end, you have both the **driver** and the **timeline**. Both go into the plan.

## What this changes about the protocol

The hormonal driver doesn't change which topicals you use. It changes:

- **The timeline.** A pregnancy-triggered melasma in someone now off the COC is a different timeline to a melasma actively driven by current HRT.
- **The seasonality.** A perimenopausal client whose flares track summer needs a tighter winter prep and a more conservative summer protocol.
- **The post-procedure call.** Active hormonal driver plus aggressive in-clinic procedure equals high rebound risk. Module 7 covers this.
- **The conversation about expectations.** A client whose driver is current and not modifiable needs to understand that the plan is **management, not cure**.
