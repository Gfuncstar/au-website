## How to use these cases

The four cases below are pattern-typical of the hyperpigmentation work that walks into UK clinics every week. They are constructed from common clinical patterns, not from any individual client's records.

For each case, work through the decisions yourself before reading the walkthrough. The aim is not to memorise an answer. It is to practise the framework from the previous nine modules in the order you would use it in chair.

---

## Case 1. Post-acne PIH on resilient skin, lower Fitzpatrick

A 28-year-old client books in for "scarring left over from her acne". Cleared the acne 18 months ago with a six-month course of doxycycline plus topical adapalene. The texture is normal. What's left is a constellation of brown macules tracking the jaw and lower cheeks where the inflammatory acne was worst.

She has tried two over-the-counter brightening serums and a vitamin C. No flare, no improvement. SPF 30, applied "most days".

### Decision walkthrough

**Presentation.** Discrete brown macules, location follows past inflammation, normal surface texture. Classic post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, epidermal.

**Depth check.** Wood's lamp shows enhancement. Epidermal.

**Driver.** Past inflammation, now resolved. No active driver.

**Plan.**

- Topical: switch to a tyrosinase inhibitor with stronger evidence (azelaic acid 15%) plus continue vitamin C in the morning.
- Add: tinted SPF (iron oxide), daily, year-round.
- Add at week four if tolerating: mandelic acid exfoliant, two nights a week.
- Add at week six if tolerating: low-strength retinoid, alternate nights.

**Timeline.** Twelve-week reassessment. Likely visible improvement at week eight, substantive at week twelve. May continue for a further twelve weeks for full clearance.

**Procedural option.** A series of mandelic peels (4 sessions, three weeks apart) starting week six if topical alone is moving slowly.

### What didn't work and why

The client's previous protocol failed because:

- The vitamin C was the only active doing the lightening work, and vitamin C alone is a slow lead.
- The SPF was inadequate for protection against background UV exposure.
- "Most days" SPF compliance lets daily UV and visible light keep stimulating melanin production.

The new protocol has a stronger lead active, a comprehensive light strategy, and a clear add-in cadence that builds tolerance before stacking.

## Case 2. Centrofacial melasma on a perimenopausal client

A 47-year-old client presents with bilateral pigmentation across the forehead, upper lip, and bridge of the nose. Symmetrical, diffuse borders, brown tone with a hint of grey on the forehead. First noticed two years ago. Worsens after holidays.

History: three pregnancies, last one fifteen years ago. Combined oral contraceptive for twenty years, stopped at 45. HRT (estradiol patch) started one year ago for vasomotor symptoms.

She has been on a clinic protocol for nine months: hydroquinone 4% with tretinoin 0.05%. Initial improvement at three months, regression at six, currently darker than baseline.

### Decision walkthrough

**Presentation.** Centrofacial melasma. Symmetrical, diffuse, classic distribution.

**Depth check.** Wood's lamp shows partial enhancement on the forehead, less on the upper lip. Mixed depth.

**Driver.** Active hormonal driver (HRT). Cumulative UV. Possible heat triggers (perimenopausal vasomotor episodes).

**Plan.**

This is the difficult case. The current protocol has been over-aggressive for the depth and the driver, and is now likely contributing to the rebound (possibly even ochronosis on the deeper element).

- **Stop hydroquinone immediately.** Long-term continuous use plus mixed-depth pigment plus active hormonal driver = ochronosis risk. Refer for dermatological review of the deeper grey component.
- **Pause the retinoid for four weeks.** Barrier rest.
- **Build back gentler.** Azelaic acid 15% as the lead, antioxidant in morning, tinted SPF (iron oxide), heat avoidance, light strategy.
- **Hormonal driver conversation.** Acknowledge HRT is current and not stopping. The plan is **management** not cure. Year-round protective protocol, not seasonal.
- **No procedural work** in summer, particularly given the rebound history.

**Timeline.** Six months minimum to first stable result. The client needs to understand this is a chronic condition being managed, not an acute condition being cured.

**Procedural option later.** Once stable, consider low-fluence 1064nm Q-switched laser by an experienced operator in autumn or winter only. Refuse IPL for this client.

### What didn't work and why

The previous clinic's protocol failed because:

- Hydroquinone plus tretinoin is the right active stack for some melasma but is too aggressive for mixed-depth pigmentation with an active hormonal driver.
- No cycling plan for the hydroquinone meant continuous use over nine months, which is the ochronosis-risk window.
- The conversation around HRT as an active driver was missed, so expectations were set as if the driver would stop being a factor.

## Case 3. Solar lentigines on a 56-year-old client, lower Fitzpatrick

A 56-year-old client presents with multiple discrete brown macules on the cheeks, temples, dorsal hands, and chest. Defined borders, irregular but flat, varying sizes. No symmetry. Lifetime of beach holidays plus thirty years of tennis.

She wants them "gone for an event in eight weeks".

### Decision walkthrough

**Presentation.** Solar lentigines. Multiple, discrete, photo-distribution.

**Depth check.** Epidermal. Wood's lamp shows clear enhancement.

**Driver.** Cumulative UV. No systemic driver.

**Plan.**

This is the easiest of the four cases, but the eight-week timeline is the constraint.

- **Procedural-led plan.** Light-based treatment (IPL or Q-switched 532nm) targeting individual lesions. Two sessions, four weeks apart, scheduled to allow full recovery before the event.
- **Pre-treatment topical for two weeks.** Antioxidant plus tyrosinase inhibitor to prime and prevent post-procedure PIH.
- **Strict tinted SPF and hat for the event window** to avoid driving more pigment in the recovery period.

**Timeline.** Likely substantial improvement before the event. Some lentigines will need a third session, scheduled post-event.

**Caveat to set.** Some recurrence is likely over the years if sun exposure continues. Maintenance is part of the plan, not a one-and-done.

### Why this case is straightforward

Epidermal pigment without a systemic driver responds well to light-based work. The lentigines are anatomically isolated, making targeted treatment efficient. No active hormonal driver means no rebound risk from the trigger side. The eight-week timeline is tight but achievable for a meaningful before-and-after.

## Case 4. PIH on higher Fitzpatrick following a peel that went wrong

A 35-year-old client, Fitzpatrick V, presents with extensive brown patches across both cheeks and the nose. Tracks the area where she had a "TCA peel" at another clinic six weeks ago. The peel went deeper than expected. The post-peel skin scabbed. The pigment is the result.

History: no melasma, no acne, no prior pigmentation problems. Combined contraceptive for ten years.

### Decision walkthrough

**Presentation.** Iatrogenic PIH from over-aggressive procedural work.

**Depth check.** Currently mostly epidermal with concern about deeper deposition given the depth of the original peel.

**Driver.** Inflammation has resolved, but the epidermis is still recovering.

**Plan.**

- **Step one: barrier first.** Two weeks of barrier-only (gentle cleanser, ceramide moisturiser, tinted SPF, nothing else).
- **Step two: gentle reintroduction.** Niacinamide 5% in week three. Azelaic acid 15% in week four if tolerating.
- **Step three: tyrosinase inhibition + protection.** Build to azelaic plus a vitamin C plus tinted SPF year-round.
- **No procedural work for a minimum of six months.** The skin needs to fully recover before any further intervention.
- **Conversation about the previous clinic.** The client may want to know if she has redress. That's outside your scope, but it is appropriate to acknowledge that the original procedure was not matched to her Fitzpatrick.

**Timeline.** Six to nine months for substantive clearance. Some risk of long-term residual pigment, particularly in the most affected areas.

**Procedural option later.** Once skin is fully calm, consider low-fluence 1064nm or fractional non-ablative by an operator with experience on Fitzpatrick V. Not before.

### What didn't work and why (at the previous clinic)

A TCA peel on Fitzpatrick V skin without adequate priming, depth control, and aftercare is a high-risk procedure. The combination of uncontrolled depth plus high baseline melanin plus inflammatory recovery is the highest-likelihood scenario for PIH. The right call would have been a much lighter peel, in series, with priming.

This is a case where the right move is **slow and patient correction**, not aggressive new intervention. The instinct to "fix it faster" with another procedure is exactly the wrong move.

## What the four cases teach

- The framework from Modules 1 to 8 isn't theoretical. Each case is a different combination of presentation, depth, driver, and history.
- The plan follows the case, not the other way around.
- Refusing or deferring procedures is part of the work. So is referring out.
- The hardest cases are the ones with active drivers and previous over-treatment. Those are also the ones where a clear, calm, framework-driven approach changes the client's experience.
