## Why "stronger" is the wrong question

The question most clients arrive with is "what's the strongest brightening serum I can use?" The right answer, almost always, is the wrong question.

Hyperpigmentation responds to **sustained gentle pressure on the melanocyte**, not to short bursts of aggressive activity that flare the skin and trigger more pigment. The single most reliable predictor of a successful twelve-week protocol is not the potency of the lead active. It's **tolerance**: a barrier intact enough to keep the actives on the face long enough to work.

This module is the topical layer, sequenced.

## The four families of active

### 1. Tyrosinase inhibitors (the lead actives)

Tyrosinase is the enzyme that converts tyrosine into the melanin precursors. Inhibit it, and the melanocyte makes less pigment. These are the workhorses.

**Hydroquinone (2–4% prescription, sometimes higher off-label)**

The most studied, most predictable lightening agent. UK prescribing is restricted; over-the-counter hydroquinone is no longer permitted in the EU and UK retail market. Use within prescribing scope only.

- Pros: Strong evidence, fast results when tolerated.
- Cons: Cycling required (typically 3–4 months on, 1–2 months off) to avoid ochronosis. Irritation common. Not for long-term continuous use.

**Azelaic acid (10–20% OTC and prescribing)**

The most underused active in UK pigmentation work. Tyrosinase inhibition plus mild anti-inflammatory plus comedolytic. Strong evidence for melasma and PIH. Excellent tolerance across Fitzpatrick types.

- Pros: Tolerable on most barriers. Helpful where rosacea overlaps.
- Cons: Slower than hydroquinone. Mild stinging in the first two weeks for some clients.

**Tranexamic acid (topical 2–5%, also oral by prescription)**

A relative newcomer to the topical pigmentation toolkit. Particularly useful in melasma. Modulates plasmin and reduces UV-induced melanocyte signalling.

- Pros: Helpful where hydroquinone is contraindicated or has been cycled off.
- Cons: Newer, fewer head-to-head trials. Compounding inconsistent across UK suppliers.

**Kojic acid (1–4% OTC)**

Mild tyrosinase inhibitor. Useful as a second-line or in combination products.

- Pros: Tolerable.
- Cons: Less potent. Sensitisation can develop with long-term use.

**Arbutin (alpha and beta forms, 2–7% OTC)**

A naturally occurring hydroquinone derivative. Slower release than hydroquinone, gentler on the barrier.

- Pros: Gentle.
- Cons: Slower. Better as a maintenance active than a lead.

**Niacinamide (4–10% OTC)**

Reduces melanosome transfer from melanocyte to keratinocyte. Doesn't stop production, slows distribution.

- Pros: Excellent tolerance. Pairs with almost everything.
- Cons: Modest effect alone. Best in combination.

### 2. Exfoliants

Speed up the turnover of pigmented keratinocytes. Helpful in epidermal pigment, used cautiously in dermal and mixed.

**AHAs (glycolic, lactic, mandelic)**

- **Glycolic.** Strong exfoliation, can flare melasma if over-used. Reserve for resilient skin.
- **Lactic.** Gentler, hydrating profile. Good for melasma and dry skin.
- **Mandelic.** Larger molecule, slower penetration, well-tolerated on higher Fitzpatrick types. Often the best choice for cautious melasma protocols.

**BHAs (salicylic acid)**

Lipid-soluble. Useful where pigmentation overlaps with congested or acne-prone skin. Less directly relevant to non-acne pigmentation.

**PHAs (gluconolactone, lactobionic acid)**

Largest molecules, slowest penetration. Excellent tolerance. Useful as a daily exfoliant in clients whose barrier won't support AHAs.

### 3. Retinoids

Increase keratinocyte turnover, normalise differentiation, support collagen, mild tyrosinase modulation.

**When they help pigment**

- Lentigines and photo-pigment respond well.
- PIH on resilient skin tolerates a slow ramp.
- Used in combination with hydroquinone (the modified Kligman triple), retinoids accelerate result.

**When they trigger PIH**

- A retinoid started too fast on a sensitive or higher-Fitzpatrick face can cause an irritation cascade and leave PIH on top of the original pigment.
- Active rosacea or compromised barrier — defer.

**When to delay them**

- Active melasma flare.
- First six weeks of a new protocol if barrier is fragile.
- Summer, particularly for melasma clients (or maintain at lowest tolerated frequency).

**Sequencing tip:** retinoid plus tyrosinase inhibitor at night, antioxidant and SPF in the morning. Introduce the retinoid two to four weeks after the tyrosinase inhibitor, not on day one.

### 4. Antioxidants

Reduce the oxidative cascade that drives pigment formation downstream of UV exposure. Best applied in the morning, under SPF.

- **Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid 10–20%, plus stable derivatives).** Strong evidence as a daytime adjunct to a pigmentation plan. Reduces UV-induced pigment formation. Pair with SPF.
- **Resveratrol, polyphenols, niacinamide, ferulic acid.** Multi-antioxidant formulations cover a wider radical profile than vitamin C alone.

Antioxidants are the **insurance policy** of a pigmentation plan, not the lead active.

## Sequencing the protocol

A reasonable starting protocol for a moderate melasma client, week one:

**Morning:**
1. Gentle cleanser.
2. Vitamin C antioxidant (if tolerated).
3. Tinted SPF with iron oxides.

**Evening:**
1. Gentle cleanser.
2. Tyrosinase inhibitor (azelaic acid or compounded option per prescribing).
3. Moisturiser.

**Add at week three to four (if tolerating):**
- Mild exfoliant 2–3 nights per week (mandelic or PHA).

**Add at week six to eight (if tolerating):**
- Retinoid, alternate nights, lowest tolerated strength.

**Reassess at week eight.** If barrier is calm, consider stepping up. If barrier is irritated, step down or pause the most recent addition.

**Reassess at week twelve.** This is the milestone where photo evidence and the client conversation come together. Plan the next twelve weeks around what's actually moved.

## The eight-week barrier rebuild

A meaningful proportion of clients arrive at consultation already over-exfoliated by previous protocols. Their barrier is broken. Putting any pigmentation active on a broken barrier triggers more pigment.

The rebuild:

**Week one to two:** stop everything except a gentle cleanser, a barrier-supportive moisturiser, and a tinted SPF. Yes, that's it.

**Week three to four:** introduce a mild antioxidant (niacinamide is the safest first re-entry).

**Week five to six:** introduce the gentlest tyrosinase inhibitor (azelaic acid is usually first choice).

**Week seven to eight:** if tolerating, introduce a mild exfoliant once or twice a week.

After eight weeks, the barrier is back, and the protocol can begin properly. The client has lost no time — they were going backwards before.
