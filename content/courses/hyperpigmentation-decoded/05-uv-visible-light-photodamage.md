## Why SPF alone fails most pigmentation plans

The most common mistake in hyperpigmentation, after misreading the presentation, is under-prescribing the light-protection layer.

A typical scenario: client has melasma. Practitioner gives them a tyrosinase inhibitor and a "broad spectrum SPF 50". Client uses both. Pigment lifts through winter. June arrives. The pigment comes back in two weeks.

The protocol didn't fail. The light strategy did.

This module is the light strategy that actually holds.

## What the sun is doing to a hyperpigmentation client

There are three light-driven mechanisms relevant to hyperpigmentation:

### UVB (290–320nm)

The wavelength that **burns** and drives most acute photoaging. UVB triggers DNA damage in keratinocytes, which signals melanocytes to produce more pigment. It is the dominant trigger in lentigines and a contributor to PIH.

Most chemical and mineral SPFs cover UVB well at SPF 30+.

### UVA (320–400nm)

The wavelength that **doesn't burn** but penetrates deeper, all the way into the dermis. UVA drives:

- Long-term photoaging
- Melanocyte hyperactivation in melasma
- Dermal pigment deposition and depth shift over years

Critically: **UVA passes through window glass**. A client driving regularly, sitting near a sunlit window, or working by a window all day is being dosed with UVA every day, no beach required. This is why some melasma clients flare in winter despite "not being in the sun".

UVA protection is what the **PA+ rating** (Asia) or **UVA seal** (EU) measures. SPF number alone tells you UVB. Always check for UVA coverage.

### Visible light (400–700nm), particularly High-Energy Visible (400–500nm)

The wavelength range most often missed in light strategies. Visible light, especially the blue end (400–500nm), **directly stimulates melanocytes** in higher Fitzpatrick skin types and in melasma.

Visible light is emitted by:

- The sun (year-round, in any weather)
- Window glass does not block it
- Phone, laptop and TV screens (lower dose, but cumulative for indoor-heavy clients)
- Some fluorescent and LED office lighting

**Conventional SPFs do not block visible light.** Most chemical SPFs are transparent to visible light by design. The only reliable visible-light protection in clinical use is **iron oxides** in tinted formulations, often paired with non-nano zinc oxide.

If your melasma client is on an untinted clear SPF, their visible light dose is almost equivalent to wearing nothing. This is the single highest-leverage change you can make at the consultation.

## What to recommend, by presentation

### For melasma

- **Tinted SPF with iron oxides**, applied generously, every day, year-round. The tint does the visible-light work.
- **Reapply every two hours** when outdoors or near windows.
- **Wide-brimmed hat and UPF-rated clothing** during summer and any travel to higher UV regions.
- **Avoid hot showers, saunas, hot yoga** during active flares — heat sensitises the same melanocyte.
- **Reframe the conversation**: this is year-round protection, not summer protection.

### For PIH

- Tinted SPF helpful but **not as critical** as for melasma if the client is lower Fitzpatrick.
- The bigger driver is **preventing further inflammation** — barrier care, gentle protocol, no over-exfoliation.
- A barrier-broken face will pigment more on the same UV dose than an intact barrier. Module 5 covers active sequencing.

### For solar lentigines / epidermal photo-pigment

- SPF and avoidance are the long-game plan, but the existing pigment can clear with treatment regardless.
- The conversation is about **preventing the next twenty years of accumulation**, not undoing the last forty.

### For dermal and mixed pigment

- Protective measures slow further deposition, but won't reverse what's already in the dermis.
- The plan is procedural plus protective.

### For PIE (the "not actually pigment" case)

- Standard SPF.
- The bigger conversation is **vascular and barrier**, not lightening.

## The summer protocol, the winter protocol

Hyperpigmentation plans need to flex with the UV year, particularly for melasma.

### Winter (October to March in the UK)

- Higher actives tolerated. Retinoids, AHAs, slightly more aggressive tyrosinase inhibition.
- Procedures (peels, microneedling) tend to be safer because UV recovery window is longer and sunlight is weaker.
- **Tinted SPF still daily**. Visible light is year-round, even on grey days.

### Spring transition (April, May)

- Begin scaling actives back. Retinoids to alternate nights or paused.
- Increase SPF reapplication discipline.
- **No new aggressive procedures** booked into late May / June without a clear reason.

### Summer (June, July, August)

- Lowest active load. Maintenance only. Tyrosinase inhibitor at low concentration. Antioxidants.
- **No peels, microneedling, or laser** in active melasma clients during peak UV unless there's a specific clinical reason and full informed consent about rebound risk.
- Tinted SPF, hat, sunglasses, behavioural sun avoidance.

### Autumn (September)

- Begin re-introducing actives. Slowly.
- Reassess the pigment after summer. Some flare is normal.
- This is the right window to plan procedures for late autumn / winter.

## The conversation that gets the SPF actually used

The biggest reason SPF compliance fails is not laziness. It's that the client doesn't understand **why** they need it indoors, year-round, on grey days.

A useful script:

> The pigment on your face is being made by a cell that gets switched on by daylight, not just the sunshine you can feel. Visible light from windows, screens and ordinary daylight switches it on too. The tinted SPF blocks the wavelength the cell responds to. If you skip it on grey days or indoors, you are giving the cell a daily reason to keep making pigment. The plan we're building cannot outwork that.

It reframes SPF from "summer skincare" to "the part of the protocol that has to happen every day or the rest of it is wasted".
