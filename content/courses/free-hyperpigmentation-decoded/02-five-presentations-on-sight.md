## Why one protocol does not fit five conditions

The biggest mistake in hyperpigmentation is treating it as one condition. It isn't. What practitioners call "pigmentation" is at least five distinct presentations, each with a different driver, a different depth, and a different protocol.

Read the wrong one and the next twelve weeks goes nowhere.

This lesson is the framework you can run in the first thirty seconds of any new consultation.

## The five presentations

### 1. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)

Pigment left behind after inflammation. Acne is the most common cause, but anything inflammatory can leave it: rashes, peels that went too deep, mechanical trauma, aggressive retinoids.

**Tells:**
- Sits where the inflammation sat (jawline, cheeks, hairline if acne-driven)
- Discrete macules tracking individual lesions
- Darker and more persistent on higher Fitzpatrick types
- Surface texture usually normal

**Depth:** Mostly epidermal, can drop deeper if untreated for years.

**Plan implication:** Gentle is better than strong. Aggressive treatment restarts the inflammation and deepens the pigment.

### 2. Melasma

Patterned, often symmetrical hyperpigmentation driven by hormones, sun and visible light. Most common in women aged 20–50. Notoriously prone to rebound.

**Tells:**
- One of three distribution patterns: **centrofacial** (forehead, upper lip, chin, nose), **malar** (cheekbones), or **mandibular** (along the jaw)
- Symmetrical
- Diffuse rather than discrete
- Worsens after sun, pregnancy, contraceptive change

**Depth:** Often mixed. Some epidermal, some dermal. The mix is what makes it difficult.

**Plan implication:** Long game. Year-round protection. IPL is almost always the wrong call.

### 3. Solar lentigines and photo-induced epidermal pigment

The "age spots" and diffuse mottled pigment from cumulative UV exposure.

**Tells:**
- Defined borders
- Cheeks, temples, dorsal hands, chest
- Pattern follows lifelong sun exposure
- Often appears alongside textural sun damage

**Depth:** Epidermal. Responds well to surface treatment.

**Plan implication:** Of the five, this is the one most likely to clear with one or two well-sequenced procedures.

### 4. Dermal and mixed pigment

Pigment that has dropped beneath the epidermis into the dermis, or sits across both layers. The presentation most often missed at consultation.

**Tells:**
- **Greyish or bluish tone** rather than clean brown
- Less response to topicals than expected
- Drug-induced pigmentation (minocycline, antimalarials, NSAIDs, amiodarone) tends to live here

**Depth:** Dermal or mixed.

**Plan implication:** A topical alone won't reach this. Two-phase plan needed.

### 5. Post-inflammatory erythema, and the conditions that get treated as pigment

The presentation most often **mis-treated**. PIE is red, vascular shadow left behind after inflammation, particularly in lower Fitzpatrick types. Looks like pigment but isn't melanin. Treating it as melanin makes it worse.

There are also conditions that walk in as "pigmentation" but aren't: rosacea, ochronosis, lichen planus pigmentosus, melanocytic naevi. Some of these need referral, not treatment.

**Tells:**
- Red, not brown
- Or doesn't fit any of the four melanin patterns above

**Plan implication:** If it's PIE, the plan is **vascular and barrier**, not lightening. If it's something else, **diagnose first, treat second**.

## How to use this in clinic

In the first thirty seconds of a new consultation you are looking for:

1. **Distribution.** Patterned (melasma) or scattered (PIH, lentigines)?
2. **Borders.** Discrete (lentigines, PIH) or diffuse (melasma, mixed)?
3. **Tone.** Brown (epidermal melanin) or grey-blue (dermal melanin)? Red, not brown (PIE)?
4. **What is the surrounding skin doing?** A face that is also rosacea-prone changes the plan.

You don't have to lock the diagnosis in thirty seconds. You have to know which of the five tracks the consultation is running so the rest of the conversation is the right shape.

## Why depth changes everything

Two clients walk in with brown pigment on their cheeks. Same Fitzpatrick. Same age. One responds to a topical in twelve weeks. The other doesn't move on the same protocol over six months.

The difference is depth. Epidermal pigment is reachable. Dermal pigment isn't, by topicals alone. The same surface looks the same to the eye but lives in different tissue layers.

A simple test in clinic: a **Wood's lamp** held about 10cm from the face in a darkened room. Epidermal pigment becomes more pronounced under the UV. Dermal pigment becomes less pronounced. Mixed shows mixed enhancement.

The Wood's lamp gives you, in twenty seconds, the answer to the most important question of the consultation: **how deep is this?**
