## Why a fifteen-minute history changes the plan more than any tyrosinase inhibitor

The strongest active in the world will not work if the protocol is matched to the wrong driver.

The hyperpigmentation client whose plan finally holds is almost never the client who got a "stronger serum." It is the client whose practitioner asked the seven questions below at the consultation, surfaced the systemic driver, and built the plan around it.

This lesson is those seven questions, and the printable card you can take into your next appointment.

## The seven consultation questions

Run these in order. Fifteen minutes total.

### Question 1. When did you first notice the pigment?

Anchor the timeline. Recent (under twelve months) often points to a specific trigger. Long-standing (years) means the original driver may have shifted and the pigment may have layered into deeper depths over time.

### Question 2. What was happening in your life around that time?

The most useful question in the consultation. Open. Lets the client surface the trigger themselves.

Listen for: pregnancy, contraceptive change, HRT, perimenopausal symptoms, period of stress or sleep disruption, a holiday with significant sun exposure, a new procedure or skincare regime.

### Question 3. Hormonal history.

Specific. Pregnancies, contraception (which formulations, when started, when stopped), HRT, cycle pattern, perimenopausal symptoms, thyroid history, family history of thyroid disease.

This is the most likely place the systemic driver shows up, particularly for melasma.

### Question 4. What have you already tried?

Their full skincare and treatment history. Past procedures. Past prescriptions. Past products. **What flared the pigment versus what didn't move it.**

This tells you their tolerance baseline and what's already been ruled in or out.

### Question 5. What does a typical week of sun and light exposure look like?

Indoor versus outdoor. Office near a window. Driving. Holiday frequency. Current SPF habit. **Tinted versus clear.** Applied once and forgotten, or reapplied. Phone screen time.

The single most overlooked driver in melasma is everyday visible light exposure (windows, screens, ordinary daylight). A clear SPF blocks UV but not visible light.

### Question 6. What's your goal, and what would success look like?

The client's frame, not yours. "I want it gone" is different from "I want it not to flare again." Knowing the target shapes the timeline conversation.

### Question 7. What's your timeline appetite?

Does the client need this resolved before a wedding in three months, or are they prepared for a year-long project? The mismatch between "want it gone next week" and the realistic twelve-week minimum is the biggest source of disappointment in pigmentation work.

If a client's timeline is unrealistic, **say so before they pay**.

## What you walk out with

By the end of those seven questions, you should be able to answer:

- **Which of the five presentations** is this?
- **What's the likely depth** (epidermal, dermal, mixed)?
- **Is there an active systemic driver** that needs to be in the plan?
- **What's already failed** for this client?
- **What's the realistic timeline?**

That is the foundation a hyperpigmentation protocol is built on. Everything else, the topicals, the procedures, the cadence, the photography, follows from those answers.
