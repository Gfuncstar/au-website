Rosacea is rarely just about redness. To treat the condition properly, you have to know which **version of it** is in front of you, and what's actually driving it.

## A working definition

Rosacea is a **chronic inflammatory facial skin condition** with vascular, neurological, immunological, and barrier components. It typically affects the central face, cheeks, nose, chin, and forehead, and presents in **four recognised subtypes**, often overlapping in the same client.

Most clinic plans treat the symptom (redness). The strongest plans treat the **underlying condition**, which is almost always a barrier-and-microbiome story before it's an inflammation story.

## The four subtypes

Each subtype tells you something different about the underlying drivers and the treatment that will actually hold.

### 1. Erythematotelangiectatic rosacea (ETR)

Persistent central facial redness, easy flushing, visible telangiectasia. The most common presentation, and the one most often mistaken for "sensitive skin".

### 2. Papulopustular rosacea (PPR)

Redness + persistent inflammatory papules and pustules. Often misdiagnosed as adult acne. The inflammatory component dominates here, and the treatment ladder differs significantly from ETR.

### 3. Phymatous rosacea

Skin thickening and tissue overgrowth, most commonly on the nose (rhinophyma). Often progresses slowly over years. Rare in early presentations but missed-treatment risk is high.

### 4. Ocular rosacea

Burning, itching, dry eyes; foreign-body sensation; recurrent styes. Frequently under-recognised in private cosmetic settings, and one of the clearest signals that a client needs ophthalmology referral.

**Important:** Subtypes are not mutually exclusive. Many clients have features of two or three at once. The treatment pathway changes based on which is dominant.

## Why "anti-redness first" doesn't hold

Most regimens for rosacea treat the redness, anti-inflammatory creams, gentle cleansers, niacinamide, azelaic acid. They reduce the surface signal, but the underlying condition continues. The barrier stays compromised. The microbiome stays altered. The vascular reactivity stays unchanged.

Within weeks, the redness comes back. The plan didn't fail because the products were wrong, **it failed because the order was wrong**.

## Barrier-first treatment

The strongest evidence-led approach to rosacea is **barrier repair first, anti-inflammatory second**.

The skin barrier in rosacea is functionally impaired:

- Reduced ceramides and lipids
- Increased trans-epidermal water loss (TEWL)
- Altered microbiome (especially *Demodex folliculorum* density in PPR)
- Heightened reactivity to stimuli that don't trouble healthy skin

A defensible plan starts by **stabilising the barrier**, then layers in subtype-specific anti-inflammatories (topical metronidazole, ivermectin, azelaic acid; oral doxycycline in some cases). Without that order, every plan is fragile.
