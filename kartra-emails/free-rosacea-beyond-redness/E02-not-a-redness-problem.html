If you read Lesson 1, you'll already have a feel for where this goes.

Most rosacea plans calm the redness for a few weeks and then drift. The client comes back warmer, more reactive, and the regimen that worked in March is suddenly making things worse in June. The practitioner blames the season, the client blames the practitioner, and nobody blames the framing, which is where the problem actually sits.

Rosacea is not a redness problem. It's a barrier-and-microbiome condition with vascular, neurological and immunological drivers underneath it. The redness is the symptom that's most visible, not the condition that needs treating. NICE recognises four subtypes, erythematotelangiectatic, papulopustular, phymatous, ocular, and each one needs a different pathway. A regimen built around "calm the redness" treats none of them properly.

Lesson 2 takes you through the four subtypes and the moment of clinical reasoning that decides which one is sitting in front of you.
