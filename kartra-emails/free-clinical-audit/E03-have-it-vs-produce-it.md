# E3 — "I have it" vs "I can produce it" (Day 4)

**Subject:** `The gap the audit was built to surface`

**Preheader:** `Having the document and producing it on demand are two different things. The audit shows you where the gap actually sits.`

---

## Body

Hi {first_name},

A confession. The first time I ran an audit like this against my
own clinic, I gave myself credit for documentation I'd written and
then never opened again.

The audit is uncomfortable because it draws a hard line between
two things most practitioners count as the same: "I have it" and
"I can produce it on demand, dated, current, consistent across
clients." Those are different states. The first one feels
reassuring; the second one is the one inspectors, insurers and
complaint reviewers actually care about.

Most of what the audit surfaces isn't missing documentation. It's
documentation that exists in some form, somewhere, and would not
hold up under scrutiny if asked for at short notice. Consent forms
that are out of date. Risk assessments that haven't been reviewed
this year. Aftercare leaflets sitting on the laptop instead of
inside the client record. A complaints procedure that lives in
your head.

That gap is where reputations get lost. Not in scandal, in
ordinary clinic life, when one client makes one complaint and the
practitioner suddenly has 48 hours to assemble a paper trail that
should have been there all along.

By the time you've completed the audit, you'll have an honest
list. Some sections green, some sections amber, some red. That
list is the most useful piece of work most clinics will ever do
for themselves, and it's free.

What the audit can't do is hand you the templates and the
governance system to close the gaps. That's a different piece of
work. I'll write again in a couple of days about it.
